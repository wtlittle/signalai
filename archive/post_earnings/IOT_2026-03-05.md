# Samsara Inc. (IOT) — Q4 FY2026 Post-Earnings Note
**Reported: 2026-03-05 (After Market Close)** | Written: 2026-03-23 | Analyst: Buy-Side Research

---

## 1. The Setup

Going into Q4 FY2026, Samsara was one of the most debated stocks in enterprise software — a company whose fundamentals were quietly accelerating while its stock had been viciously de-rated. From its peak in early 2025, IOT shed roughly 57% over twelve months, trading as low as $23.38 at its 52-week trough, hitting the mid-$20s by late January 2026. The selloff was not idiosyncratic — the iShares Expanded Tech-Software ETF fell ~21% from its highs over the same stretch — but Samsara underperformed even that broader carnage. The bear argument was straightforward: a premium-valued, loss-making IoT platform selling to physical operations companies (trucking, construction, waste management) at a 7.8x forward P/S, in a macro environment where fleet and operations-tech budgets were under scrutiny, run by insiders who had been collectively selling hundreds of millions of dollars worth of stock. The CEO and co-founder Sanjit Biswas and CTO John Bicket each sold 263,900 shares at ~$25.87 in the prior quarter alone; total insider sales over the prior three months approached $92.9M — a hard number to wave away.

The institutional debate heading into Q4 centered on three questions. First, could net new ARR growth genuinely re-accelerate after years of deceleration as the business scaled? The company had delivered 24% net new ARR growth in Q3 and guided Q4 revenue to $421-$423M — implying roughly 22% reported growth, a meaningful step-down from prior cadence. Bears read that as proof that the law of large numbers was finally catching up with the platform. Bulls countered that Q3's guidance had been similarly conservative, and management consistently guided to de-risked scenarios. Second, was the emerging product contribution (20% of net new ACV in Q3) a real second engine of growth, or were Asset Tags and AI Multicam still too small to move the needle at scale? Third, when would the profitability inflection become durable? Q3 had delivered Samsara's first-ever GAAP profitable quarter, but it had been quietly celebrated given that Q4 guidance implied a sequential step-down in non-GAAP operating margin. Sell-side consensus EPS for Q4 was $0.13; revenue consensus sat at $422.3M. The stock entered earnings day on March 5 at roughly $28-29, having lost ~19% year-to-date versus the S&P 500's approximately flat performance — a setup that implicitly demanded more than a modest beat to move the needle.

Goldman Sachs had initiated coverage just days before the report with a Buy and a $45 price target — a notable endorsement but also a signal of how deeply the stock had been abandoned by the institutional complex. KeyBanc had cut its price target to $40 from $55 in late February. Piper Sandler had trimmed to $37. Truist had a Hold with a $30 target. The pre-earnings narrative from the most skeptical corners was that IoT/physical-operations digitization was a "nice to have" not a "need to have," that the transition from SMB to enterprise would bring longer sales cycles and lumpier quarters, and that the path to sustained GAAP profitability was still uncertain. The stock was priced for more disappointment. It got the exact opposite.

## 3. The Story of the Quarter

The headline numbers are impressive, but the real story of Q4 FY2026 is that three independent growth vectors fired simultaneously — and not because of any single promotional period, deal pull-forward, or accounting quirk. The quarter had the texture of genuine, broad-based acceleration.

**Large enterprise momentum was the primary driver.** Net new ARR of $144.8M was 33% above the prior year and represented the third consecutive quarter of sequential acceleration — the highest growth rate in eight quarters. Critically, this was *not* driven by SMB; it was driven by the biggest logos getting bigger and new $1M+ ACV wins at a quarterly record pace of 13 deals. For context, Q3 had tied the prior quarterly record at 17 $1M+ *ARR* customers added, but Q4 was specifically 13 net new ACV deals above $1M — a more demanding threshold. Highlighted wins included Southern California Edison, Groundworks, Harris County (Texas's third-largest county), and a top-10 win with Estes — the largest privately held freight company in North America with 43,000 trailers and 10,500 tractors. The Estes deal illustrates exactly what the bull case looks like in practice: they started with video-based safety and telematics, then expanded in Q4 to add Equipment Monitoring, Asset Tags, and Connected Asset Maintenance. That is platform land-and-expand working as designed. ARR from $1M+ customers grew 56% YoY — the third consecutive quarter of sequential acceleration at a larger scale. The concentration of ARR toward 100K+ customers continued its multi-year drift upward, reaching 61% of total ARR from 58% a year ago and 56% two years ago.

**Emerging products crossed a meaningful milestone.** At 23% of net new ACV in Q4 (up from 20% in Q3 and ~8% a year ago), and with emerging product ARR crossing $100M for the first time, the second revenue engine is no longer a rounding error. Eight of the top 10 net new ACV deals included an emerging product — meaning the biggest logos are adopting the new suite, not just the core telematics platform. Asset Tag ARR more than tripled year-over-year; Samsara signed its largest-ever Asset Tags deal with Total Safety (250,000+ tracked assets). The company also launched the Asset Tag XS — five times smaller than the original — purpose-built for handheld tools, medical equipment, and gas meters, expanding the addressable universe well beyond heavy fleet. The company's integration with Hubble's 90M+ smartphone network extends indoor visibility, addressing a longstanding limitation of GPS-only tracking. Importantly, CFO Dominic Phillips emphasized that no single emerging product drove more than 50% of the new product ACV — this is distributed product adoption across AI Multicam, Asset Maintenance, Asset Tags, Commercial Navigation, Routing, Qualifications, Training, and Workflows. That breadth is exactly what durable platform companies exhibit at maturation.

**Management's narrative on the macro environment was notably confident.** Rather than offering the standard "we see some elongation in sales cycles" caveats, Dominic Phillips highlighted that Samsara's value proposition is tied to *physical operations budgets* — which constitute approximately 80% of customers' total revenues. In other words, Samsara is not competing for discretionary IT capex; it is embedded in the operating cost structure of companies that move goods, build infrastructure, and manage equipment. The CFO explicitly called out alignment with the *AI infrastructure build-out* as a tailwind — citing that the stock price of Samsara's top 100 public customers had risen more than 30% over the prior year, reflecting the capital expenditure surge in data centers, power infrastructure, and logistics that is directly relevant to Samsara's construction, wholesale, and public sector verticals. This is a subtle but important reframe: Samsara is not just a fleet management SaaS company; it is infrastructure software for the companies building the physical backbone of the AI economy.

**The AI agent announcement was understated but strategically significant.** The launch of the AI Safety Coach — an autonomous agent that reviews dashcam footage, conducts real-time voice coaching in the cab, and auto-adjusts following distances based on weather conditions — represents the first product in what management calls "Phase Three" of their three-phase digitization framework. It is noteworthy because it is not simply an analytics dashboard or a better alert system; it is an automated workflow that replaces a task previously performed by fleet managers. Management outlined a roadmap of additional agents for compliance, maintenance, and dispatching. With 25 trillion data points flowing through the platform annually — from hundreds of millions of miles of daily road footage — Samsara has a proprietary training corpus that is structurally difficult to replicate. The data network effect (more assets → better AI models → more customer value → more customers) is the kind of competitive moat that is easy to say and hard to demonstrate; this quarter, it showed up in the financial results.

## 4. Why the Stock Reacted the Way It Did

IOT surged roughly 11.5% in after-hours on March 5 and extended gains to approximately +16-20% by the end of March 6 trading — its best single-day performance since the IPO, according to Simply Wall St. The move was not driven by a single factor but by a simultaneous resolution of all three of the pre-earnings debates. Net new ARR at $144.8M (33% growth, third consecutive quarter of acceleration, highest in eight quarters) answered the growth durability question definitively. GAAP EPS of $0.04 for the second consecutive quarter answered the profitability durability question. And non-GAAP EPS of $0.18 versus a $0.13 consensus — a 38.5% beat — plus revenue $22M above consensus resolved any remaining doubt about near-term execution.

The more durable source of excitement, however, was the *guidance* rather than the backward-looking beat. Management guided Q1 FY2027 revenue to $454-$456M — roughly $10M above the prior Street consensus of ~$444M, representing 24% YoY growth. Full-year FY2027 revenue guidance of $1.965B-$1.975B came in meaningfully above the pre-earnings consensus of approximately $1.91B — a ~3% beat to full-year estimates at the initiation point. Crucially, management guided non-GAAP EPS for FY2027 at $0.65-$0.69 versus a Street consensus that had been sitting around $0.33-$0.57 — a 100%+ beat to the EPS line. Most importantly, management explicitly guided for *full-year GAAP profitability* in FY2027 — a milestone that had previously been expected only in FY2028. That pulled forward a key narrative inflection point by at least one year and forced the market to re-engage with what the Samsara earnings power profile actually looks like at scale.

Contrast this with the Q3 reaction: Q3 delivered the first-ever GAAP profitable quarter and strong ARR growth, but the Q4 revenue guidance ($421-423M) was only marginally above consensus (~$419M), and the stock barely moved after hours. The difference in market reaction between Q3 and Q4 was almost entirely about the magnitude of the forward guidance beat. Q4's guidance was unambiguous: not a "whisper-beat," not a guidance reset lower, but a meaningful step-up in expected ARR velocity and profitability. Management also added a notable "downside case" floor: net new ARR in FY2027 should be "at least as much as in FY2026" — i.e., at least $432M — even in pessimistic scenarios. That is a rare form of explicit downside protection in guidance language, and sophisticated investors noticed. For a stock that had been cut in half on growth fears, the combination of a real beat and real upside guidance was the setup for exactly the squeeze that followed.

## 7. Thesis Impact

**The bull thesis has materially strengthened — this was a thesis-defining quarter.** For a buy-side analyst with a 1-3 year horizon, Q4 FY2026 addressed the most structural concern about Samsara: whether growth would re-accelerate or slow as the company scaled. The answer was unambiguous — three consecutive quarters of accelerating net new ARR growth, culminating in the highest growth rate in eight quarters, achieved at a $1.9B ARR base. This is not a company coasting; the growth engine is genuinely re-accelerating. The mechanism — enterprise land-and-expand with multi-product depth — is exactly the type of SaaS motion that compounds durably. The fact that 96% of 100K+ ARR customers use two or more products, 69% use three or more, and nine of the top 10 Q4 deals included two or more products (six included four or more) is the best possible configuration for an ARR business: customers that are deeply embedded across workflows are not switching vendors.

**The TAM penetration story is in the earliest innings.** Management's framework — three phases of physical operations digitization (connect, analyze, automate), with most of the world's physical operations still in Phase 1 or 2 — is credible and supported by Q4 data. The $25K+ ARR customer cohort contributes 85% of total ARR (up from 81% two years ago), but this still represents a tiny fraction of the addressable fleet and asset management universe. Samsara's top 10 verticals include sectors — construction, freight, waste management, wholesale/retail, public sector — that are collectively enormous and still largely paper-and-spreadsheet-driven. The construction vertical has now contributed the highest net new ACV mix of all industries for 10 consecutive quarters, and had its highest quarterly growth in seven quarters in Q4. Public sector ARR growth accelerated for the third consecutive year. Europe ARR growth accelerated for the fourth straight quarter. The multi-geography, multi-vertical acceleration profile is exactly the pattern that sustains growth for years beyond consensus model horizons.

**Bull/bear framework post-Q4:**

*Bull case (~$55-65 price target, 2-3 year)* — Net new ARR continues to compound at 25%+ as emerging products become a second revenue engine, large enterprise adoption deepens, and AI agents (Phase 3) unlock a meaningfully higher per-seat ACV. Non-GAAP operating margins expand from 17% (FY2026) toward 25-30% as the business scales on a largely fixed infrastructure base. GAAP profitability arrives a year early (FY2027), and multiple expansion follows as the market re-rates from a "growth-at-loss" profile to a "growth-at-profit" profile. At $2.3B+ in ARR exiting FY2027 and 25-30% operating margins, Samsara is worth 15-18x NTM revenue — implying a $60-75 stock in 3 years.

*Bear case (still ~$30-35)* — Revenue growth decelerates back toward 15-18% in FY2028 as the enterprise market gets picked over and competitors (Motive, Verizon Connect, Geotab, J.J. Keller) improve their AI capabilities. Tariff-driven hardware cost pressures erode gross margins below 75%. Insider selling continues to dampen sentiment. Non-GAAP operating margins plateau in the 17-20% range rather than scaling toward 30%+. The stock continues to trade at 8-9x forward revenue, in-line with mid-growth SaaS peers.

*What tips the balance:* Q1 FY2027 results (in ~June 2026). If the company delivers $455M+ and net new ARR at or above Q4's $144M, the re-rating is real and likely self-reinforcing. If net new ARR decelerates back toward $110-120M, bulls will need to revisit the thesis.

## 8. Follow-Ups (Actionable)

1. **Monitor Q1 FY2027 net new ARR (June earnings)** — The single most important number. Management's "at least $432M net new ARR" full-year floor implies $108M/quarter on average; Q4 was $145M. Is Q1 closer to $125M (seasonal step-down) or back toward $110M (re-deceleration)? This will determine whether the re-acceleration is durable.

2. **Track emerging product ARR breakout** — With emerging products crossing $100M ARR and contributing 23% of net new ACV, watch for management to begin disclosing individual product ARR (Asset Tags in particular) at the Investor Day (June 23-26, Las Vegas). The asset-tracking market alone (competing with AirTag networks, Tile, Bluetooth sensor ecosystems) is substantially underpenetrated.

3. **Attend/review the Beyond 2026 Investor Day (June 23-26)** — This is the first formal Investor Day in years and will likely include a TAM expansion discussion, Phase 3 AI agent roadmap, and potentially a multi-year financial model. Could be a significant re-rating catalyst.

4. **Watch AI Safety Coach adoption metrics** — The first AI agent in the platform. Track whether Q1 call mentions specific adoption rates or additional AI agents launched for compliance, maintenance, or dispatching. Phase 3 agent monetization (premium ACV uplift) is the unmodeled upside.

5. **Evaluate insider selling trajectory** — Net selling of ~$92M in the prior quarter was significant. Watch whether insider sell schedules moderate post-earnings as confidence in the re-rating grows. A reduction in insider selling pressure would be a meaningful positive sentiment signal.

6. **Build sensitivity to tariff / hardware cost scenario** — Management mentioned tariff risk in guidance modeling notes. With Asset Tags and IoT hardware sourced from contract manufacturers (likely Asia-exposed), a tariff escalation could compress gross margins. Management noted they believe they are "best-positioned and best-capitalized to navigate through this," and explicitly framed tariffs as a potential market share opportunity, but build a 75-77% non-GAAP gross margin scenario into models (vs. management's 78% target).

7. **Update competitive intelligence on Motive** — Private competitor Motive (formerly KeepTruckin) remains the most credible head-to-head challenger in fleet telematics. Any reports of Motive fundraising, revenue disclosures, or customer wins in Q1-Q2 should be incorporated into competitive positioning. Samsara's 13 $1M+ deals are enterprise-grade; if Motive is landing similar-scale enterprise deals, it warrants reassessment.

8. **Consider position sizing relative to the macro beta** — IOT has a beta of 1.60. In a broad tech selloff, the stock will bleed faster than the index regardless of fundamentals. Given the March 2026 macro backdrop (tariff uncertainty, early AI capex concerns), sizing should account for continued volatility. The Q4 print materially de-risked the fundamental thesis; the macro risk has not disappeared.
