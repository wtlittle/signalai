# McCormick & Company (MKC) -- Post-Earnings Note
**Reported:** March 31, 2026 (Before Open, 8:00 AM ET) | **Quarter:** Q1 FY2026 (Dec–Feb) | **Day 12 Post-Earnings**

---

## The Setup

McCormick entered the print at ~$53, down 21% since its Q4 FY2025 report, near multi-year lows, with a 52-week range of $47.52–$78.16. The prior-quarter miss ($0.86 vs $0.87 est) had punctured sentiment, compounded by insider selling (~115K shares, $7.95M). Market cap at report was ~$14.3B, P/E ~18x trailing. The McCormick de México acquisition (closed late 2025) was the key variable: would it contribute meaningfully in Q1, or would integration costs overwhelm the top-line benefit?

Consensus was cautious: $0.59–$0.61 EPS, $1.78–$1.79B revenue. In the "Deflation Risk" macro regime — VIX elevated, consumer staples bid for safety, gas >$4, Iran war escalating oil above $100 — MKC's defensive Consumer Staples profile should have appealed. But the stock needed a genuine beat to recover from its 21% drawdown. The expectation was modest: no one was modeling a +16.7% revenue quarter.

There was a second reason to watch this print closely: long-running speculation that McCormick could be an M&A target or acquirer had circulated given the valuation compression.

## The Story of the Quarter

McCormick delivered a strong Q1 on the numbers, but the earnings call was completely dominated by a major strategic announcement: **McCormick agreed to combine with Unilever Foods in a Reverse Morris Trust transaction** — one of the largest deals in the history of the consumer staples sector.

**Q1 Operating Performance (the minority of the call):**

Revenue of $1.874B (+16.7% YoY) was driven primarily by the McCormick de México acquisition contribution. CEO Brendan Foley described growth in both Consumer and Flavor Solutions segments, with the acquisition adding meaningful inorganic revenue. Organic volume growth was flat YoY (versus 2.2% growth in Q1 FY2025), reflecting a consumer environment where volumes are not yet fully recovering. Pricing contributed modestly.

Adjusted EPS of $0.66 beat consensus by $0.05–$0.07, driven by acquisition accretion and cost discipline through the CCI (Comprehensive Continuous Improvement) program. Operating margin of 12.1% was down 190bps YoY — integrating McCormick de México is creating near-term cost friction. FCF margin fell to ~1% from 4.9% in Q1 FY2025, reflecting working capital absorption from the integration. This is expected and not alarming, but worth tracking.

Full-year adjusted EPS guidance of $3.05–$3.13 was reaffirmed. Organic sales growth guidance of 1–3% was maintained. No guidance raise despite the Q1 beat — a typical McCormick pattern of conservative management, though it also signals that Q2–Q4 integration costs and macro headwinds are visible.

**The Dominant Story: McCormick + Unilever Foods**

The bulk of the earnings call was spent announcing and explaining a transformational combination. McCormick agreed to combine with Unilever Foods in a **Reverse Morris Trust** structure. Key deal terms:

- **Transaction structure:** MKC issues a fixed number of new shares as consideration for Unilever Foods
- **Ownership post-close:** ~65% Unilever shareholders / ~35% McCormick shareholders
- **Cash to Unilever:** $15.7 billion (funded via debt + existing capacity)
- **Enterprise value implied for Unilever Foods:** ~$44.8B
- **Enterprise value implied for McCormick:** ~$21B
- **Combined EBITDA multiple:** ~13.8x CY2025 EBITDA for both companies (based on 1-month VWAP)
- **Pro forma annual revenue:** ~$20B
- **Pro forma operating margins:** ~21% (best-in-class for consumer staples)

**The brands McCormick gains from Unilever Foods:**
Knorr (bouillon, seasonings), Hellmann's (mayonnaise), French's (mustard), Frank's RedHot, Cholula (hot sauce), Maille (French mustard/condiment), and a suite of regional brands across EMEA, Latin America, and Asia Pacific. Together, the combined company would own the #1 or #2 position in virtually every major flavor and condiment category globally.

**Strategic rationale:**
Four growth levers were identified: (1) expanded distribution — McCormick's North American retail execution + Unilever's emerging market scale; (2) scaling high-growth brands (Frank's RedHot, Cholula, Maille) across new geographies via Unilever's distribution infrastructure; (3) foodservice: McCormick's front-of-house branded presence + Unilever Food Solutions' back-of-house depth creates a ~$6B pro forma foodservice platform; (4) innovation at scale — combined R&D capabilities in flavor science, emulsion technology, and culinary development.

**Cost synergies:**
$600M annual run-rate cost synergies by year 3, expected ~2/3 captured by year 2. Identified across procurement, media, manufacturing, logistics, and SG&A. McCormick's CCI program will be applied to the combined entity.

**Financial impact:**
- Expected to be accretive to growth, adjusted operating margin, and adjusted EPS in the first full year
- By year 3: sustainable organic sales growth of 3–5%, operating margins of ~23–25%
- Net leverage at or below 4x at close; targeted reduction to ~3x within 2 years
- Dividend maintained — both companies have ~60% historical payout ratios
- McCormick remains HQ'd in Hunt Valley, Maryland
- Combined company: international HQ in the Netherlands (where Unilever operations are significant)
- Brendan Foley (CEO) and Marcos Gabriel (CFO) continue in their roles

**Fernando Fernández (Unilever CEO) rationale:** This transaction is Unilever's exit from its Foods division as part of its strategic reshaping toward a "pure-play home and personal care company." Unilever shareholders receive 35% participation in the upside of the combined global flavor leader.

**The governance angle:** Unilever receives two years of board representation at McCormick, maintaining alignment during the integration period.

## Why the Stock Reacted

Despite the double-beat in Q1 and the potentially transformational deal announcement, MKC stock did not react positively. From the April 4 prior note context: the stock had rallied to ~$56.66 in early trading on March 31. However, by April 2 close, MKC had fallen to $48.85 — well below both the pre-earnings level of ~$53 and the March 31 early-trading high.

**Detailed price action:**
- Mar 30 (day before): Closed ~$52.56
- Mar 31 (report day, before open): Last 15-min bar closed ~$50.41 — fell during the session
- Apr 1 (Day 1): Closed ~$50.40 — essentially flat
- Apr 2 (Day 2): Closed $48.85 (+0.97% on the day vs -12.4% from pre-earnings levels)
- 52-week low as of Apr 2: $47.52

The stock's reaction was paradoxical given what appeared to be a clean beat. The likely drivers:

1. **Reverse Morris Trust structure is dilutive.** Issuing shares such that Unilever shareholders end up with 65% of the combined company means MKC shareholders are immediately diluted from 100% to 35% ownership of a much larger entity. At $15.7B of new debt layered on top, the balance sheet goes from comfortable to leveraged (net leverage 4x+). Markets initially priced in the dilution and leverage risk.

2. **Q1 organic volume flat, not recovering.** The beat was acquisition-driven, not organic. For a company that was supposed to be demonstrating volume recovery in the back half, flat volumes raised questions about the underlying demand environment.

3. **Guidance not raised.** Despite a $0.05–$0.07 EPS beat in Q1, management maintained the full-year range rather than raising, suggesting Q2–Q4 integration costs will consume the Q1 upside.

4. **Macro pressure on staples.** The broader market selloff driven by Iran war escalation, oil >$115, and tariff fears pressured Consumer Staples names even as they held up relatively better. MKC at $49 is not immune.

5. **Near-term EPS dilution optics.** Post-deal, the combined company's FY2026 EPS will be substantially different from standalone MKC guidance ($3.05–$3.13). Investors who bought MKC on a standalone basis face uncertainty about the new financial profile until more deal details emerge.

## Thesis Impact

**The thesis has transformed.** This is no longer purely a "defensive staples holding recovering from valuation compression" story. MKC is now a **transformational M&A story** with a 12–18 month regulatory and integration timeline.

**The bull case (strengthened on a 3–5 year view):**
- Creating a preeminent global flavor powerhouse with Knorr, Hellmann's, McCormick, French's, Frank's RedHot, Cholula, and Maille is strategically compelling — no other company owns this breadth of global flavor leadership
- $600M annual cost synergies by year 3, on top of existing CCI, suggests a path to 23–25% operating margins — best-in-class for the sector
- Organic sales growth of 3–5% by year 3 (vs. current 1–3%) reflects genuine scale benefits
- The $6B pro forma foodservice platform fills a gap McCormick has never been able to address as a standalone company
- First-year accretion to adjusted EPS is the key near-term financial proof point
- Foley/Gabriel leadership continuity reduces execution risk
- Flavor as a category is structurally advantaged: it is the #1 purchase driver in food, aligned with health/wellness trends and Gen Z cooking-at-home behavior
- McCormick's 137-year history of disciplined M&A integration (Cholula, French's, ReckittBenckiser foods) provides credibility

**The bear case (real and valid):**
- 4x+ net leverage at close is the most significant risk — if revenue growth disappoints post-integration, debt service constrains capital allocation flexibility
- $15.7B of new debt in a high-rate environment is costly; deleveraging to 3x within 2 years requires strong FCF generation and disciplined execution
- Regulatory risk is non-trivial: combining the world's largest herb/spice brand with Unilever's condiments portfolio will attract scrutiny in the EU, UK, and potentially US markets; remedies (brand divestitures) could reduce the deal's strategic value
- 65/35 ownership structure means existing MKC shareholders are highly diluted; the math only works if the combined entity creates substantial value beyond the standalone
- Integration complexity: Unilever Foods operates across dozens of markets with deeply embedded local supply chains and go-to-market systems; McCormick has never managed an integration of this scale
- Standalone organic volume growth of 0% in Q1 FY2026 is a cautionary signal — if the underlying core business isn't growing volumes, the deal must compensate through synergies alone
- Operating margin contraction of 190bps in Q1 signals near-term integration friction will precede the promised margin expansion
- Guidance maintenance (not raised) after a 10.9% EPS beat suggests headwinds are building in Q2–Q4

**Insider activity:** Former CEO Lawrence Kurzius sold 50,000 shares at $67.43 in January 2026 — well above current levels (~$49). Multiple other executive-related transactions (F-INKIND, M-EXEMPT) in February reflect routine compensation activity. No open-market buying by insiders at current depressed levels.

**Positioning:** MKC at $48.85 is now a complex investment. The standalone thesis (defensive staples yield, acquisition accretion from de México) has been subsumed by a deal that will take 12–18 months to close and 3+ years to prove out. Short-term, the stock faces overhang from dilution optics and leverage concerns. Long-term, if management executes the integration and delivers the $600M cost synergy program and 23–25% margins on a $20B revenue base, this is a substantially more valuable company than the standalone. The risk is that integration complexity, regulatory remedies, or macro deterioration delay or diminish the strategic case.

## Follow-ups

1. **Regulatory review timeline** — EU and UK merger review of MKC + Knorr + Hellmann's + French's is the critical path item. Watch for regulatory filing dates and any early signals on remedies required.
2. **Deal close estimate** — Management guided 12–18 months. Any acceleration or slippage materially changes the financial timeline.
3. **Pro forma financial model** — When McCormick files more detailed deal financials (S-4 or proxy), the revised EPS framework will clarify year-1 accretion reality.
4. **Q2 FY2026 organic volume** — Does volume recover from Q1's flat print, or does macro deterioration push it negative?
5. **FCF normalization** — FCF margin fell to ~1% in Q1; watch for recovery in Q2–Q3 as de México integration costs ease.
6. **Standalone guidance raise** — Will management raise FY2026 adj. EPS guidance at Q2 despite the deal (both are tracked separately until close)?
7. **Synergy roadmap detail** — Watch for investor day or analyst day presentation on the specific $600M synergy breakdown (procurement vs. SG&A vs. manufacturing).
8. **Leverage trajectory** — Track quarterly debt reduction progress toward the 3x target; any miss vs. plan is a negative catalyst.
9. **Brand divestitures** — If regulators require any brand remedies, the strategic and financial impact of the deal changes.
10. **Flavor category trends** — Monitor organic volume in herbs/spices and condiments for signs of consumer spending degradation in the Iran war / high-gas-price macro regime.
