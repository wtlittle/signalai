## The Story of the Quarter

The headline EPS beat of $1.23 vs $0.76 was spectacular on the surface -- an 86% YoY increase. However, the quarter included a $2.8B termination fee from the Warner Bros. Discovery content deal, recognized under "interest and other income." This one-time item materially inflated both earnings and free cash flow ($5.1B FCF vs $2.7B prior year).

Stripping out the WBD fee, the underlying business was still strong: revenue grew 16.2% YoY to $12.25B on higher subscriptions, elevated pricing, and growing ad revenue. The ad-supported tier now accounts for 60%+ of sign-ups in markets where it is available, and the advertising business is on track for ~$3B in 2026 revenue (2x 2025).

Netflix has stopped reporting quarterly subscriber numbers, making it harder to assess engagement trends. Management said results were "slightly above internal forecasts" due to stronger-than-expected membership growth and favorable FX.

## Why the Stock Reacted

Despite the massive EPS beat, NFLX shares fell approximately 9% in after-hours trading on April 16, continuing to decline ~10.8% to $96.20 by Friday morning. The sell-off was driven by soft Q2 guidance and the realization that the EPS beat was heavily influenced by the one-time WBD fee. The market was also unsettled by the lack of subscriber transparency and concerned about content spending as the WBD deal unwind creates programming gaps.

Full-year guidance was maintained at $50.7-51.7B revenue and 31.5% operating margin, which the market viewed as conservative but not inspiring.

## Guidance and Tone

Management maintained FY 2026 guidance: revenue $50.7-51.7B, operating margin ~31.5%. The ad business is on track for $3B in 2026. The tone was positive but not aggressive. Key concern: the absence of subscriber disclosures and somewhat cautious Q2 commentary created uncertainty. The market wanted a guidance raise after such a strong Q1, and didn't get one.

---

## Thesis Impact

**Neutral.** The underlying business is healthy -- 16% revenue growth, 32%+ margins, and a rapidly scaling ad business. But the EPS beat was quality-distorted by the WBD fee, and the 9-10% stock drop signals that the market was positioned for a guidance raise that didn't come. The stock remains a "premium growth" name that needs to demonstrate subscriber momentum without the subscriber disclosure.
