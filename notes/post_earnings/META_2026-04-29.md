## The Setup

META entered Q1 with elevated expectations after a strong 2025 in which AI-driven ad performance pushed Family of Apps revenue above expectations every quarter. The Street modeled revenue of ~$55.5–55.7B and adjusted EPS of $6.65–6.85, with the central debate concentrated on **the trajectory of full-year 2026 capex**. Management's January guide of $115–135B was already unprecedented; the buy-side question was whether the Q1 print would surface enough commercial AI proof points to justify maintaining (or expanding) that envelope. The macro backdrop was mixed — the regime had shifted toward Slowdown, the VIX was elevated, and the Mag 7 trade had decoupled, with capex-heavy hyperscaler stories being punished while asset-light AI beneficiaries (GOOG, NFLX) were outperforming.

## The Story of the Quarter

The advertising machine ran hotter than at any point in the company's history. Family of Apps generated **$55.91B of revenue and $26.90B of operating income** — a 48% segment operating margin. The drivers were two-sided: **+19% impression growth** signals continued user-engagement gains (Threads, Reels, AI-powered feed ranking), and **+12% price per ad** confirms that AI-driven ad-targeting improvements are translating to ROAS gains advertisers are willing to pay for. The combination produced the strongest top-line print in Meta's history in absolute dollar terms.

Reality Labs continued to bleed but with two new wrinkles: **revenue declined 2% YoY to $402M** (vs. growth expected) as Quest headset volumes softened more than glasses growth could offset, while the operating loss of **$4.03B** was modestly better than the $4.21B loss in Q1 2025 — the first quarter of YoY loss improvement since the segment was broken out. Cumulative RL losses since 2020 now exceed $90B. Management announced an **8,000-person headcount reduction** (effective May 20) concentrated in RL and middle-management ad-stack roles; the savings will be reinvested in AI infrastructure rather than dropping to the bottom line.

The single number that defined the print was **the capex raise**. Management lifted full-year 2026 capex guidance to **$125–145B** (midpoint $135B) from the prior $115–135B (midpoint $125B), citing higher memory component pricing and additional data-center commitments for future capacity. Critically, **full-year expense guidance was unchanged at $162–169B** — meaning operating margin remains intact in 2026, with the capex pressure landing on depreciation in 2027–2028.

Cash generation remained robust: operating cash flow of ~$25B in the quarter and $81.18B in cash and marketable securities at quarter-end. The buyback authorization remains substantial, though pace will moderate as capex absorbs incremental free cash flow.

## Why the Stock Reacted

META fell **~7–9% in after-hours** on April 29 and has been range-bound around **$640–680** in the four sessions since (vs. pre-print close of ~$745):

1. **Capex shock dominated.** The $10B incremental midpoint raise was larger than buy-side whisper numbers ($130B central case). With NVDA Q1 GTC ramping and memory pricing structurally higher (HBM3e/HBM4 supply tight), the market is now modeling **$160B+ as a credible 2027 capex run-rate**, which translates to ~$50B+ of incremental annual depreciation by 2028 — a meaningful headwind to operating income at current margin levels.

2. **Tax-benefit-adjusted earnings beat was narrower.** Excluding the one-time $8.03B tax benefit, adjusted EPS of $7.31 still beat by ~7%, but the headline GAAP figure inflated the apparent quality of the print. Quant funds flagged this immediately.

3. **Reality Labs revenue miss reignited the abandonment debate.** Quest momentum is decelerating, and despite "tripling" of daily AI glasses users (from a small base), absolute revenue declined 2%. The 8,000-person layoff was read as a tacit acknowledgment that the metaverse thesis remains unproductive at current spend levels.

4. **Q2 deceleration optics.** Q2 guide implies +24% YoY at midpoint vs. +33% in Q1. Bulls argue this is conservative seasonality; bears note that Q2 2025 was a tougher comp and that the deceleration into +20% range would compress the multiple.

5. **Sector rotation context.** The session brought a broader hyperscaler de-rate — GOOG and AMZN traded heavy on capex concerns, while asset-light names (NFLX, TTD) outperformed. Meta caught a sympathy selldown beyond its idiosyncratic capex headline.

**Tone:** Mark Zuckerberg's prepared remarks were aggressive on AI infrastructure — he framed the capex as "a once-in-a-generation buildout" and emphasized that **agentic products and business AI conversations are early-stage but tracking ahead of internal models**. CFO Susan Li was measured, repeatedly pointing out that operating margins (41% in Q1) provide significant cushion to absorb depreciation step-ups. The Q&A pushed back on the "ROI on capex" question; management declined to provide explicit GPU-utilization or ARPU-from-AI metrics, which the buy-side read as defensive.

## Thesis Impact

**For longs:** The core ad business accelerated to its highest growth rate in 2.5 years (+33%). AI-driven ad performance is real, measurable in price per ad (+12%), and has not yet saturated. Cash generation is enormous, balance sheet is fortress (~$81B cash). Q2 guidance came in above consensus despite a deceleration. The capex raise, while uncomfortable, is concentrated in compute that will service the same advertising stack that just printed +33%. If AI-driven monetization sustains through 2026, the multiple compression today will reverse on outyear earnings power.

**For shorts/skeptics:** $145B capex implies $50B+ of incremental annual depreciation by 2028, which compresses OI margins by ~500–700bps from the 41% Q1 base. Reality Labs continues to bleed with no clear path to monetization despite layoffs. The +33% revenue growth is partly cyclical (easy 2025 comps) — Q2's +24% midpoint shows the trajectory normalizing. Capex visibility is poor: management explicitly said memory pricing was a key driver of the raise, suggesting **further raises are possible** if HBM4 supply tightens.

**Net thesis impact:** Mixed. The fundamental ad business is healthier than at any point since 2021. The capex envelope is genuinely concerning at the marginal level — $135B vs. consensus $125B is a $40B+ NPV drag at 8% WACC. The setup over the next two quarters depends on whether **AI agentic monetization** (new ad inventory, business-AI conversations, value optimization) can produce visible revenue streams by Q3/Q4 to validate the buildout. We are tilted neutral with a slight defensive bias — preferring to add on weakness toward $620 (where the stock would trade at ~22x adjusted 2026 EPS, in line with the long-term mean).
