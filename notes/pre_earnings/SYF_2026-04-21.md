## 2. Key Debates & Variants

**Debate 1: Delinquency Inflection — Has the Normalization Cycle Truly Peaked?**
Q4 2025 was the clearest evidence yet that SYF's post-pandemic credit normalization is behind them: NCO of 5.37% vs. 6.45% a year prior, delinquencies below pre-pandemic benchmarks. The critical Q1 2026 test: does this favorable trend hold as the economy absorbs tariff shocks and macro uncertainty? SYF's portfolio skews to non-prime and near-prime borrowers, who are more sensitive to employment and inflation shocks. Management's FY2026 NCO guidance of "5.5–6%" (within long-term targets) was set assuming 4.8% year-end unemployment and 2% GDP growth. If Q1 2026 macro data has deteriorated (trade war concerns, tariff pass-through to prices), the NCO baseline assumption comes under pressure. Bears: NCO could re-accelerate to 6%+ by H2 2026.

**Debate 2: The 10% Rate Cap — Is the Legislative Threat Real?**
This is the most persistent overhang on SYF stock. In January 2026, the Trump administration discussed a 10% APR cap on credit cards. If enacted, it would fundamentally break risk-based pricing and force SYF to tighten credit significantly for millions of subprime/near-prime borrowers, gutting interest income. However: (1) the proposal has faced substantial legal opposition, (2) the Credit Card Competition Act (CCCA) remains separate and has not advanced, (3) the banking lobby has been effective in delaying such measures. JPMorgan characterized this as "high-severity, low-probability" in January. The Q1 print gives SYF an opportunity to reiterate that no regulatory changes have materialized in the guide.

**Debate 3: Purchase Volume Growth — Can SYF Sustain Trajectory on a Tight Credit Posture?**
Q4 2025 purchase volume of $49B was a fourth-quarter record (+3% YoY), despite net credit restrictive actions taken mid-2023 to early 2024. Dual/co-branded card volume grew 16% YoY. The Q1 2026 question: does volume growth continue to reaccelerate as newly launched programs (Lowe's Commercial Co-Brand, Walmart One Pay, Versatile Credit acquisition) ramp? The Lowe's Commercial Co-Brand transfers to SYF's portfolio in Q2 2026 — this is a material catalyst. SYF also added 25+ partners in Q4, including Bob's Discount Furniture and Polaris renewal.

**Debate 4: Net Interest Margin Sustainability**
SYF's Q4 2025 NIM expanded to 15.83% (+82bps YoY), driven by PPPCs (product, pricing, policy changes) that implemented higher APRs and fees *in anticipation of* the CFPB late-fee cap (which was subsequently vacated). The "double benefit" — higher rates AND the late-fee cap not materializing — created a favorable Q4 2025 NIM. The Q1 2026 dynamic: NIM will begin to face slight pressure from (1) lower benchmark rates (if Fed cuts) reducing funding costs partially but also variable-rate asset yields, and (2) the "new account yield dilution" effect as SYF grows its portfolio with newly originated accounts that carry lower rates initially. SYF guided NIM to remain supported in 2026 but acknowledged some headwinds from lower late-fee incidence and new account ramp.

## 3. What Matters This Print

**1. Net Charge-Off Rate (Q4 2025: 5.37%; Q1 2026 Est. ~5.6–5.8%)**
Q1 is seasonally the weakest quarter for credit (post-holiday delinquencies convert to charge-offs). The critical benchmark: SYF's own Q1 2025 NCO was 6.38%. If Q1 2026 prints below 6.0% — and ideally in the 5.5–5.8% range — it would confirm the credit normalization trajectory is intact. Any print above 6.0–6.2% raises the bear case that macro headwinds have reignited the delinquency cycle.

**2. 30+ Day Delinquency Rate (Q4 2025: 4.49%; Q1 2025: 4.52%)**
The leading indicator. Q1 delinquencies should be sequentially elevated vs. Q4 (seasonality), but the year-over-year comparison is where the thesis either validates or breaks. If SYF prints 30+ delinquency below Q1 2025's 4.52% for the second consecutive year, that is a powerful signal that the credit normalization cycle is durable.

**3. FY2026 EPS Guidance Confirmation or Revision ($9.10–$9.50)**
This is the most watched single data point for medium-term investors. Given macro deterioration risk (tariffs, uncertainty), does SYF hold its $9.10–$9.50 guide? Any cut to the guidance range — even a modest one — would be severely penalized at the current multiple. Any raise (or tightening toward the top half) would be a material catalyst. Management's FY2025 guidance was reliably maintained throughout the year.

**4. Purchase Volume & Loan Receivables Growth**
FY2026 guidance called for *mid-single-digit ending receivables growth*, accelerating in H2. Q1 purchase volume growth rate (vs. Q4's +3%) and new account adds signal whether the credit tightening drawdown is ending. Watch for Walmart One Pay performance, which began ramping in Q4 2025, and any early Lowe's ramp commentary.

**5. RSA Ratio and Net Revenue Margin**
Retailer Share Arrangements (RSAs) represent the profit-sharing mechanism with SYF's partners. Q4 RSA was 4.3% of average receivables (above 4.0–4.5% target, reflecting strong program performance). An elevated RSA rate is mathematically negative for SYF's reported revenue (it represents income shared with partners) but signals strong program economics. Any sign of RSA trending below 4.0% (from better credit performance improvement moderating) or above 4.5% (from unexpected credit stress) would inform the 2026 earnings trajectory.
