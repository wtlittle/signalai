# MKC Pre-Earnings: Q1 FY2026 (Reports March 31, 2026)

> **Reports:** Tuesday, March 31, 2026, 8:00 a.m. ET | **Presenters:** Brendan Foley (CEO), Marcos Gabriel (CFO), Faten Freiha (IR)
> **Consensus EPS:** $0.61 adj. | **Consensus Revenue:** ~$1.79B | **Current Price:** $53.23

## Set-up

MKC enters Q1 reporting sitting at **$53.23**, down **-36% from its 52-week high of $83.15** and trading at a trailing P/E of approximately **18.2x** (TTM EPS $2.93 reported, $3.00 adjusted). Year-to-date the stock has shed roughly **-21%** from its January 2 open of ~$67.68, making it one of the worst performers in consumer staples in 2026. Volume has run at nearly **3x the 30-day average** (~10.4M vs. ~3.7M avg) in the past week, suggesting active repositioning rather than passive selling. The market cap now stands at ~$14.3B — a notable contrast to the reported $32–37B valuation of Unilever's Foods business that McCormick has bid on.

The consensus narrative entering this print is deeply pessimistic, and it crystallized around three overlapping events: (1) the January 22 Q4 FY2025 earnings call, which delivered a soft FY2026 EPS guide of **$3.05–$3.13** and flagged ~$50M in incremental gross tariff costs — triggering an immediate **-8.1%** reaction; (2) a further **-5.5% drop on March 10–11** after a research-provider downgrade to "strong sell" resurfaced margin durability concerns; and (3) a **UBS price-target cut to $59 from $67** on March 20 — just ahead of this report — as the analyst updated their model pre-earnings. Sell-side consensus is squarely neutral-to-cautious: out of 7 firms with current ratings, **3 are bullish, 4 are neutral, 0 are bearish** (by rating label), yet average/median price targets of **$73.57/$71** imply ~38% upside from current levels, suggesting the market has run well past what even cautious analysts anticipated. The stock is now trading at a significant discount to its own fair-value estimates across multiple firms (Simply Wall St: ~$73.31; BofA target: $80; JP Morgan target: $82; Bernstein target: $93).

The market appears to be pricing in a worst-case combination: organic growth stalls near 1% (the bottom of guidance), gross margin recovery fails to materialize, and the Unilever Foods deal — if consummated — dilutes MKC shareholders into a structurally more leveraged, less-pure-play business. Positioning is crowded to the downside: the stock's RSI recently dropped to ~21, technically oversold territory, and institutional holders have been trimming. The **key question for March 31** is not whether McCormick beats Q1 consensus — it is whether management reaffirms full-year guidance and provides credible evidence that the gross margin recovery trajectory is on track.

## Key Debates and Variants

- **Tariff pass-through credibility and commodity basket inflation.** Management guided to ~$50M in *incremental* gross tariff costs for FY2026, with mid-single-digit total COGS inflation expected (similar to 2025, when they reduced net tariff impact from ~$70M to ~$20M via re-sourcing and pricing). McCormick sources ~17,000 ingredients from 80 countries; key exposed commodities include black pepper, cinnamon, vanilla, and garlic — none of which are commercially scalable in the U.S. The September 2025 pricing actions in Americas consumer (2% contribution in Q4) are just beginning to lap. The debate: can management offset the $50M through CCI savings, alternative sourcing, and surgical pricing without triggering volume elasticity that erodes the hard-won 7-consecutive-quarter consumer volume growth? Q1 will be the first full quarter where pricing actions and tariff costs run concurrently against a weakening consumer backdrop (U.S. savings rate at ~3.5%, lowest since late 2022).

- **Consumer organic growth: volume leadership intact or peaking?** The bull case rests on McCormick's 7-consecutive-quarter run of consumer volume growth — a differentiated outcome relative to packaged food peers. Management's own guidance for FY2026 organic growth is only **1%–3%**, the lowest bar they've set in several years (CAGNY reaffirmed this range in February). The concern is that private label has now captured ~25% of unit volume in U.S. spices/seasonings — and category traffic-per-trip is declining even as trip frequency rises. Q1 consensus revenue of ~$1.79B implies approximately **11–12% reported growth** (mostly McCormick de Mexico) but only ~**1–2% organic growth** — consistent with guidance but leaving no margin for execution risk.

- **Gross margin recovery — the pivotal storyline.** FY2025 full-year adjusted gross margin was **37.9%** (down 60 bps vs. 2024's 38.5%), with Q4 FY2025 adjusted gross margin at **39.0%** vs. 40.2% the prior year (down 120 bps). Management's FY2026 guidance explicitly calls for **gross margin expansion** — a recovery from 2025 compression — as the year's primary profit driver. The Q1 hurdle is modest (Q1 FY2025 gross margin was among the weakest quarters), but sequential tariff escalation in Q1 2026 (tariff rules shifted mid-2025) and the lack of Mexico acquisition harmonization in COGS could make Q1 gross margin optics the most scrutinized line item on the P&L.

- **Flavor Solutions: stabilization vs. continued drag.** Flavor Solutions revenue grew only ~1% organic in Q4 FY2025, driven entirely by 2% pricing against -1% volume. Underlying pressures include: soft large CPG customer volumes, slow branded foodservice traffic, and a Latin America inventory reset (expected to clear in Q1 2026). The bull case is that the customer pipeline in "high-growth innovators" and private label — areas McCormick is actively winning — will accelerate mix. The CAGNY commentary highlighted that the flavors customer pipeline "has doubled relative to the prior year." If Flavor Solutions volume turns positive in Q1 for the first time in several quarters, it would be a meaningful sentiment catalyst.

- **The Unilever Foods wildcard: strategic optionality or balance-sheet risk?** The emergence of McCormick's offer for Unilever's Foods division (~$32–37B in estimated value, versus MKC's ~$14B market cap) dramatically changes the investment debate. A Reverse Morris Trust structure would result in Unilever shareholders owning >50% of the combined entity — effectively a transformative dilution event. BofA (Buy, $80 PT) sees "meaningful synergies and compelling EPS accretion." UBS cut to $59 citing "execution risk and a hefty transaction value." Barclays (Equal Weight, $67 PT) acknowledges strategic merit but warns that Unilever shareholder majority "could dampen initial investor enthusiasm." Management is unlikely to provide substantive Unilever commentary during the Q1 earnings call, but investors will probe CFO Marcos Gabriel on balance-sheet capacity, leverage tolerance, and the impact on FY2026 guidance credibility.

## Scenario Grid

### Base Case (60% probability)

**What happens:** McCormick reports Q1 revenue in-line at ~$1.79–1.82B (+11–13% reported, ~1.5–2% organic), adjusted EPS of $0.60–0.65 (slightly above the $0.61 consensus, consistent with recent pattern of modest revenue beats). Consumer segment shows volume growth for the 8th consecutive quarter, albeit modestly (+1.0–1.5%). Flavor Solutions organic growth is slightly positive or flat, confirming the Latin America inventory reset has cleared. Gross margin comes in at 38.0–38.5%, showing year-over-year improvement from Q1 FY2025 but not a dramatic recovery, with tariff and commodity costs partially offset by CCI savings and pricing. Management reaffirms full-year EPS guidance of $3.05–$3.13, notes the elevated Q1 tax rate was anticipated, and provides a measured update on the Unilever discussions without material new information (or confirms discussions are ongoing with a timeline of "weeks").

**Stock reaction:** +3% to +7%. The stock is deeply oversold (RSI ~21) and any in-line/modest beat with guidance intact should trigger technical covering and value-oriented buying. The -36% drawdown from highs has been extreme relative to underlying fundamentals — a "not as bad as feared" result recovers some of the speculative downside pressure. The Unilever overhang remains a ceiling on any sustained rally.

---

### Bull Case (20% probability)

**What happens:** Q1 revenue beats consensus by ~$50–80M on stronger-than-expected Flavor Solutions volume recovery (organic growth +2–3%) and better Americas consumer performance. Adjusted EPS prints at $0.67–0.72 — driven by gross margin expansion of 80–120 bps YoY (a clear recovery trajectory signal). Management narrows FY2026 EPS guidance to the upper half ($3.10–$3.13) or signals confidence in exceeding $3.13. CCI program savings are quantified at or above plan. Consumer private-label competition is characterized as "stable" rather than "intensifying." Either (a) the Unilever deal is formally announced with compelling accretion math, or (b) management confirms they will not pursue the deal — either removing the overhang.

**Stock reaction:** +10% to +15%. A clear gross margin recovery print combined with Flavor Solutions inflection would force consensus upgrades across a neutral-heavy analyst community. With the stock trading at a steep discount to intrinsic value estimates (7 analyst average PT: $73.57), upward price-target revisions from currently neutral firms would compound the move. A clean deal announcement with accretion clarity would push toward the higher end.

---

### Bear Case (20% probability)

**What happens:** Q1 revenue misses consensus by $30–60M on weaker organic growth (~0–0.5%) and continued Flavor Solutions pressure (organic negative). Adjusted EPS of $0.52–0.58, missing the $0.61 consensus — driven by gross margin that fails to recover (below 37.5%), with management citing "continued higher-than-expected" commodity and tariff costs (echoing Q4 language). Full-year EPS guidance is either cut (below $3.05) or qualified heavily. The Q1 tax rate headwind exceeds expectations. Management commentary on the Unilever deal raises concerns about balance-sheet strain without providing accretion clarity — potentially confirming the deal is a ~$750M all-in-stock transaction that would dilute MKC shareholders to a minority in a combined entity.

**Stock reaction:** -8% to -14%. A second consecutive guidance-miss event would break the bottom of the technical range and force a re-rating of the shares toward trough valuation (~14–15x forward earnings). Neutral analysts who have not downgraded would likely move to sell/underweight, and the Unilever deal overhang would shift from speculative-positive to structurally-negative. The -14% scenario requires both an EPS miss and a deal announcement without near-term accretion clarity.
