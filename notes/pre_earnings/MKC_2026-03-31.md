## Unilever Foods Strategic Transaction — The Elephant in the Room

> **This section covers the most significant development since the last note update. On March 20, 2026, both McCormick and Unilever confirmed they are in active discussions regarding a potential combination of Unilever's Foods business with McCormick — a potentially transformational, once-in-a-generation deal that utterly dominates the March 31 earnings call agenda.**

### Deal Overview

On March 19, 2026, *The Wall Street Journal* first reported that Unilever and McCormick were in advanced talks. On March 20, both companies issued formal statements confirming discussions. McCormick's statement read:

> *"McCormick is engaged in discussions with Unilever regarding a potential strategic transaction involving Unilever's Foods business. While these discussions are ongoing, there can be no certainty or assurances as to whether an agreement for a transaction will be reached or as to the terms or timing of any such transaction. McCormick regularly evaluates its portfolio and strategic options in pursuit of maximizing shareholder value and consistent with its fiduciary duties and in consultation with its financial and legal advisors."*

Unilever confirmed it had received an "inbound offer" for its Foods business and is in discussions, while noting the Board believes Foods is "a highly attractive business" — language suggesting Unilever is negotiating for the best possible price, not signaling distress.

WSJ sources indicated a deal could be announced by the **end of March 2026** — which would be the same week as McCormick's Q1 FY2026 earnings call on March 31.

### What Is Unilever Foods?

Unilever's Foods division is the slower-growing anchor of an otherwise fast-pivoting personal care and beauty company. It encompasses:

| Brand | Category | Geography |
|---|---|---|
| **Hellmann's** | Mayonnaise, condiments | #1 in US & Brazil |
| **Knorr** | Bouillons, soups, seasonings, pasta & rice mixes | Global (Unilever's 2nd-largest brand) |
| **Marmite** | Yeast extract / spreads | UK, Commonwealth |
| **Colman's** | Mustard, condiments | UK |
| **Horlicks** | Malted milk drinks | India, SE Asia |
| **Various sauces** | Stir-in sauces, cooking sauces | EMEA, APAC |

**2025 Foods division financials:**
- Revenue: **~$14.9B** (~25% of Unilever's total 2025 sales)
- Underlying EBITDA: **~€3.2B (~$3.5B)** (per Barclays estimate, March 2026)
- Underlying sales growth: **~2.5%** — lagging Unilever's Beauty & Wellbeing segment (+4.3%)
- CEO Fernando Fernandez, in his first year, has made clear that food is no longer a strategic priority; he is pivoting Unilever toward beauty, personal care, and wellness

Kraft Heinz was reportedly in separate talks to acquire Unilever Foods that fell through just prior to McCormick's offer emerging — suggesting McCormick moved quickly and opportunistically.

### Proposed Structure: Reverse Morris Trust

Given that Unilever Foods (~$14.9B in sales, implied equity value $33B+) vastly exceeds McCormick's current ~$14.5B market cap, a direct cash acquisition is impossible without catastrophic leverage. The proposed structure is a **Reverse Morris Trust (RMT)**, a tax-efficient mechanism:

1. **Unilever spins off** its Foods division into a standalone NewCo (tax-free to Unilever and its shareholders under IRS rules, contingent on >50% ownership condition)
2. **NewCo immediately merges** with McCormick in an all-stock transaction
3. **Critical constraint:** Unilever shareholders must own **>50%** of the combined entity post-merger (BofA, Bloomberg, Barclays all concur on this structural requirement)
4. The deal is confirmed as **all-stock — no cash consideration** (per WSJ sources)

This means existing MKC shareholders face significant dilution: if Unilever shareholders own ~55-65% of the combined entity (estimates vary), McCormick's current shareholders would own roughly 35-45% of a much larger company.

| Combined Entity (Illustrative) | Detail |
|---|---|
| Combined revenue (est.) | ~$21B (~$6.1B MKC + ~$14.9B UL Foods) |
| Implied equity value of deal | **$33B–$37B** (Bloomberg: €29B/$33B; FinancialContent: up to $37B) |
| MKC current market cap | ~$14.5B |
| Deal premium vs. MKC mkt cap | ~2.3x–2.5x |
| MKC shareholders' est. ownership | ~35%–45% of combined entity |
| UL shareholders' est. ownership | ~55%–65% of combined entity |

*Note: The RMT structure is tax-free only if executed correctly; IRS scrutiny of any "device" to avoid taxes could complicate execution.*

### Strategic Rationale for McCormick

**Bulls argue this is transformational in the right direction:**

- **Scale:** Combined ~$21B in revenue makes MKC a genuine global food powerhouse, dramatically increasing retailer relevance and shelf/aisle negotiating leverage
- **Dry + Wet:** McCormick dominates "dry" flavorings (spices, seasonings); Unilever Foods anchors "wet" condiments (mayo, sauces) — the combination creates a nearly full-suite condiment, flavor, and sauce platform
- **Hellmann's synergy with existing portfolio:** McCormick already owns Cholula (hot sauce) and French's (mustard). Adding Hellmann's (#1 US mayo) creates a formidable condiment troika
- **Knorr geography:** Knorr has massive penetration in EMEA, Latin America, and APAC — markets where McCormick is underrepresented. Knorr is Unilever's second-largest brand globally (behind only Dove)
- **Flavor Solutions upside:** Knorr's food service and professional cooking portfolio aligns with McCormick's Flavor Solutions segment, potentially creating meaningful B2B cross-sell
- **Premiumization thesis:** Hellmann's has pricing power and premiumization runway (organic, vegan variants); McCormick's brand management track record (Frank's RedHot, Cholula) demonstrates capability
- **Cowen (TD Cowen) view:** "Compelling strategic rationale... significant potential for synergies... McCormick can radically enhance operations in terms of scale, global presence, and retailer relevance" (March 20)

**As Barclays noted:** The strategic fit is described as "nearly perfect" — combining dry flavorings dominance with wet condiments leadership.

## Key Debates & Variants

### 1. McCormick de Mexico: Accretive or Dilutive to Near-Term EPS Optics?

**Bull:** The $750M acquisition (closed January 2, 2026) brings McCormick's stake in the JV from 50% to 75%, consolidating revenues previously carried as "unconsolidated income." Mexico contributed ~$750-800M in revenues (estimated) on a full-year basis. In Q1 FY2026, this is the first full quarter of consolidated revenues, adding an estimated **$185-200M to the top line** and boosting Consumer segment volume/mix. The JV was also highly profitable — management called it "accretive to net sales, adjusted operating margin, and adjusted EPS" and noted it would contribute 11-13% of the 13-17% FY2026 reported sales growth guide. The Mexico business anchors leadership in mayonnaise (high-velocity, high-loyalty) with a FIFA World Cup 2026 activation pipeline.

**Bear:** The deal adds meaningful gross debt (~$750M financed via commercial paper/available cash). Net debt post-deal is estimated at ~$4.65B vs. $3.9B pre-close, and interest expense headwinds offset a portion of the operating income accretion. Additionally, the **elimination of the 25% minority interest** in Mexico net income attributable to Grupo Herdez reduces reported earnings attribution. Management was explicit on the Q4 call that "year-over-year EPS growth is reduced by the elimination of the 25% minority interest." This mechanical drag is expected to compress EPS leverage in 2026 and 2027 before wrapping in 2028. Consensus still does not appear to fully agree on the net EPS impact of the consolidation.

**The $0.61 vs. $0.806 consensus divergence:** Multiple platforms show adj. EPS consensus at **$0.61** for Q1 (Yahoo Finance, Quiver Quantitative, Finnhub), while one aggregator (Perplexity Finance consensus tool) shows $0.806. The $0.61 figure aligns with the street's view of Q1 adj. EPS growing ~$0.01 above Q1 FY2025's $0.60 — a modest beat-of-comp print. The Q1 FY2025 non-GAAP adj. EPS was $0.64 (consensus at the time was $0.64, actual came in at $0.60, a $0.04 miss). For this preview, we anchor to **$0.61 adj. EPS** as the operative consensus bar.

### 2. Tariff Exposure & Gross Margin Recovery

McCormick guided for an incremental **~$50M gross tariff impact** in FY2026 (Jan. 22 call), flagging mid-single-digit total inflation from tariffs + commodities — similar to 2025. CFO Marcos Gabriel stated he expects to "mitigate the vast majority" through productivity savings (CCI), alternative sourcing, and surgical pricing. However, with ~50% of incremental tariffs on McCormick items still in place post-recent reductions, and consumer sentiment declining (particularly for lower-/middle-income households), the pricing lever is under scrutiny. If tariff escalation accelerates (e.g., a renewed trade flare with China or Latin America), the $50M estimate could prove too low — and gross margin recovery guidance could be at risk.

**Key variable:** Vanilla commodity pricing (meaningful cost component), pepper, and packaging costs have been volatile. The company's "broad basket" approach to commodities means no single input drives the story, but cumulative pressure matters.

### 3. Organic Growth Quality: Volume vs. Price

McCormick delivered **seven consecutive quarters of Consumer volume growth** through Q4 FY2025 — a key management anchor and differentiation point. However, volume growth in Q4 FY2025 decelerated to just +0.2% while pricing stepped up to +1.9%, as September 2025 pricing actions took effect. The market is concerned that volume elasticity will be worse in 2026 as the consumer continues to trade down. FY2026 organic guidance of **1-3%** is modest — implying pricing of ~1-2% and volume/mix of ~0-1%.

The bull case requires Consumer segment volumes to re-accelerate in Q1 as pricing laps begin to moderate. The bear case: price-sensitive consumers reduce frequency on spices/condiments, and volume goes negative for the first time in two years. Any volume decline in Consumer would be a significant negative read-through, particularly given the stock's valuation.

### 4. Flavor Solutions Recovery vs. Structural Softness

Flavor Solutions (historically ~44% of revenue, now ~40% with Mexico Consumer consolidation) has been pressured by softness in large CPG customer volumes and sluggish branded food service foot traffic. Q4 FY2025 adj. operating income was $85.8M at an **11.9% margin** — below the Consumer segment's 20.5%. For FY2025 as a whole, Flavor Solutions grew operating income 9% (11% cc), showing real margin recovery despite top-line headwinds.

Management expects Flavor Solutions performance to **strengthen in 2026** as:
- Latin America inventory reset (Q4 2025 headwind) normalizes
- Flavors customer pipeline has doubled YoY
- QSR volume remains resilient in Americas and Asia Pacific
- Large CPG volumes stabilize

**Debate:** Is the CPG customer softness structural (GLP-1 impact, private label penetration) or cyclical (inventory drawdown + macro)? The answer affects the long-term revenue model meaningfully.

### 5. CAGNY Messaging and Long-Term Algorithm Credibility

At CAGNY 2026 (February 17), management **reaffirmed the long-term algorithm** of 4-6% net sales growth, 7-9% operating income growth, and 8-11% EPS CAGR. Critically, they framed 2026 as a "bridge year" — above-normal cost headwinds (tax rate normalization, interest expense, minority interest elimination) constrain EPS growth to 2-5%, but the underlying business algorithm resurfaces in 2027/2028 as these headwinds wrap. Post-CAGNY, the stock initially rallied, then sold off again — suggesting skepticism about whether the cost bridge is a one-year phenomenon or the new baseline. This print needs to validate the bridge narrative with clean execution.

### 6. The Unilever Deal: Value-Creating Transformation or Bet-the-Company Overreach?

**This is now the dominant debate for MKC investors.** The March 20 confirmation of Unilever Foods discussions has bifurcated the investor base:

**Bull — Deal is the right move at the right time:**
- McCormick at ~$14.5B market cap is arguably undervalued; an all-stock transaction at this level means MKC is "buying" Unilever Foods using arguably cheap currency relative to combined entity intrinsic value
- Combined ~$21B revenue business has scale, geographic reach, and brand portfolio that no standalone MKC could build organically in a decade
- McCormick's integration track record (RB Foods/Frank's/French's) gives confidence in operational execution; TD Cowen calls the strategic rationale "compelling"
- Synergies (revenue: retailer leverage, cross-sell; cost: procurement, logistics) could be substantial — BofA calls them "meaningful"
- Hellmann's + Cholula + French's creates the world's leading condiment platform
- If deal is accretive to EPS at reasonable EBITDA multiples (BofA says likely), existing shareholders participate in a much larger earnings base over time

**Bear — Massive execution risk on a distressed balance sheet:**
- McCormick is currently in a "bridge year" with elevated leverage (~4.4x net debt/EBITDA), a mechanical EPS drag from Mexico minority interest, and a stock down 36% from highs — the worst possible time to absorb a $33B+ transformational deal
- Existing MKC shareholders would own only ~35-45% of combined entity — extreme dilution
- Unilever Foods grows at ~2.5% vs. MKC's stated 4-6% long-term algorithm; the deal drags on MKC's organic growth profile
- Integration of a $14.9B global food business with operations in dozens of markets is orders of magnitude more complex than anything MKC has attempted
- Barclays estimates €350-400M in separation costs alone in Year 1
- CEO Foley and CFO Gabriel have limited M&A tenure; this deal would test their leadership under the most challenging conditions
- Regulatory complexity: condiment/sauce overlaps in US (mayo, mustard, hot sauce) create FTC divestitures risk
- Deal could distract management from the organic recovery narrative and Mexico integration — both of which require near-term focus

**The critical Q31 call question:** Will management characterize the deal as "a unique opportunity at the right price" or as "maximizing long-term shareholder value" — and which framing does the market reward?

### 3. Adjusted Gross Margin
**Expected direction: Expanding vs. Q1 FY2025's 37.6%**

Management explicitly guided for full-year gross margin expansion — Q1 is the first test. Key moving parts:
- **Positive:** Mexico business consolidation (higher-margin Consumer product), CCI savings, modest pricing contribution
- **Negative:** ~$50M full-year tariff headwind (front-half weighted?), elevated commodity costs, step-up in inventory to fair value for Mexico (a GAAP-only impact)
- **Watch:** Adjusted gross margin vs. both Q1 FY2025 (37.6%) and Q4 FY2025 (39.5% adjusted). Any sequential decline from Q4 would be a negative data point, even if YoY improving.

Expect management to report adjusted gross margin (excluding inventory step-up from Mexico). Target zone for a "good" print: **38.5-39.5%** adjusted. Consensus EBITDA for Q1: ~$318M, implying EBITDA margin of ~17.8%.

### 4. Consumer Segment Organic Growth (Volume Specifically)
**Prior quarter: +3% organic (+1% volume, +2% price)**

Volume growth is the brand-health barometer. Management has highlighted seven consecutive quarters of Consumer volume growth. A sequential step-down to flat or negative volume as pricing actions (September 2025) fully anniversarize would be a significant concern. Watch for:
- **Americas volume:** Q4 was +1%; any improvement here in Q1 would be positive given the pricing headwind
- **EMEA:** Eighth consecutive quarter of volume growth in Q4; running at +1-2%
- **China:** Management guided for "gradual" China consumer recovery in 2025 and called it on-track; Q1 2026 should show progress

**Bull signal:** Consumer organic growth >3% with any volume component ≥1%
**Bear signal:** Consumer organic <1%, especially if driven by volume decline

### 5. FY2026 Guidance Reaffirmation (Or Raise)
**Current guide: Net sales +13-17%, adj. EPS $3.05-$3.13**

The key question: does management maintain, narrow-to-top, or raise the FY2026 guide? At CAGNY (Feb. 17), management **explicitly reaffirmed** all components of the guide. A reaffirmation with Q1 execution visibility would be the baseline. Any upside surprise in Mexico integration progress or tariff mitigation outperformance could support a guide-raise narrative. 

Organic sales guidance specifically (1-3%) will be scrutinized — if management narrows the range to the top half (2-3%), it would be constructive. Any reduction in the organic guide would be a major negative given that the reported growth rate is already ~90% acquisition-driven.

## Key Earnings Call Watchpoints

### Quarterly Execution Questions

1. **First-quarter tax rate disclosure** — CAGNY flagged Q1 tax rate > 24% full-year guide; by how much?
2. **Mexico revenue and margin contribution** — first full-quarter consolidated; management likely to quantify this explicitly
3. **Tariff update** — are tariffs tracking to the $50M full-year estimate? Any change to mitigation assumptions?
4. **Consumer volume trends in March** (post-quarter color) — does management feel good about the trajectory into Q2?
5. **Flavor Solutions booking/pipeline** — management said the customer pipeline in flavors has doubled; any quantification?
6. **GAAP charges** — inventory step-up from Mexico fair value accounting will be a GAAP EPS headwind in Q1/Q2; size matters
7. **Guidance cadence** — are they still at 1-3% organic, or is there any narrowing toward the top/bottom?
8. **Language on the consumer backdrop** — any incremental commentary on consumer trade-down, GLP-1 trends, or category velocity

### Unilever Deal Questions (Now Dominant Call Risk)

9. **Deal status as of March 31** — Has a definitive agreement been reached? Have discussions terminated? Management will face direct questions regardless of outcome
10. **RMT structure confirmation** — If announced, will management confirm all-stock Reverse Morris Trust structure and the Unilever shareholder >50% ownership constraint?
11. **Dilution math** — What is the exchange ratio? What do MKC shareholders own in the combined entity? How does the deal affect FY2026 and FY2027 EPS on a per-share basis?
12. **Synergy framework** — What is the initial synergy target? Timeframe to realization? Revenue vs. cost split?
13. **Combined leverage** — What is the combined entity's pro forma net debt/EBITDA? Is there a deleveraging commitment?
14. **Integration approach** — Will McCormick management run the combined entity, or is there a shared leadership structure given Unilever shareholders' majority ownership?
15. **FY2026 guidance applicability** — Does existing guidance ($3.05-$3.13 adj. EPS) remain valid in the event of a deal announcement, or is it suspended pending combined entity modeling?
16. **Regulatory timeline** — Expected regulatory review process (FTC, EU, other jurisdictions); any pre-agreed divestitures?
17. **If deal falls through** — What is the explanation? Does standalone MKC strategy revert to the organic algorithm?
