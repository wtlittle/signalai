## Set-up

JPM closed at $294.60 on Apr 2 (after-hours), down approximately **9.5% YTD** from its Jan 2 close of $325.48 and nearly 13% off its 52-week high of $337.25. The stock is trading at ~14.7x trailing EPS ($20.03 TTM). Market cap: ~$795B. The 52-week range is $202.16–$337.25, and JPM has meaningfully underperformed its YTD peak — reflecting market anxiety about the tariff/Iran stagflation scenario that directly challenges bank NII and credit quality outlooks.

Pakistan's army chief brokered an overnight framework — the "Islamabad Accord" — proposing a 45-day ceasefire between the US and Iran, with Strait of Hormuz reopening in phase one. Both sides received the proposal on Apr 5–6. **Brent has pulled back to $108.42** (from ~$115), and **VIX is at 24.88** (down from 27.61 on Apr 2). The S&P 500 opened +0.3% on ceasefire hopes. Ceasefire talks remain fragile — Iran has declined to reopen Hormuz under a "temporary" ceasefire and rejected the US's 15-point plan — but the directional shift from acute war risk is a meaningful positive for bank stocks heading into the Apr 14 print. **March CPI is due Friday April 11** — the single most important macro event of the week, with implications for the Fed rate path and JPM's NII guide.

**Macro update (Apr 7):** Regime shifted from Deflation Risk to **Slowdown** (Growth -0.50, Inflation -0.06 Neutral, Policy -0.56 Restrictive, Sentiment -0.75 Risk-Off). JPM's most recent official NII guidance update came on February 23 (8-K/Company Update): firmwide NII was raised to **~$104.5B** for FY2026 (up from $103B), with the entire $1.5B raise attributed to CIB Markets NII — not core lending — reflecting optimism on trading conditions in a higher-for-longer environment. Core NII (ex-Markets) remains ~$95B. This update is significant context: it confirms JPM's management was already signaling Q1 trading strength before the April geopolitical escalation.

JPM's Q4 2025 print (Jan 13, 2026) was mixed: revenues of $45.80B slightly missed the $46.17B consensus, while EPS of $5.23 beat the $5.01 estimate by $0.22. The stock sold off 4.2% on the day despite the beat, reflecting: (1) Dimon's cautious macro commentary on tariff uncertainty; (2) NII guidance of $103B for FY2026 (since raised to $104.5B); and (3) expense guidance of $105B for 2026 (up >9% YoY), the key overhang.

JPM is the first "must-watch" print of Q1 earnings season given its size, breadth, and Dimon's macro commentary function. Markets were closed Apr 3–5 (Good Friday); Monday Apr 7 opens with ceasefire hopes partially offsetting the Liberation Day tariff fallout. The key narrative is whether JPM's fortress balance sheet and diversification let it shine when macro uncertainty is highest — the "flight to quality" bank. The 8-K raised the firmwide NII target to $104.5B and reaffirmed a through-the-cycle 17% ROTCE target and a 2026 Card Services NCO rate of ~3.4%.

## Key Debates and Variants

- **NII trajectory — the $104.5B guide (raised from $103B on Feb 23):** JPM officially raised its FY2026 firmwide NII to ~$104.5B in February, with the entire uplift from CIB Markets. Core NII ex-Markets is still guided at ~$95B. With rate cuts pushed further out (futures pricing only ~1 cut in 2026), the deposit margin compression thesis has reversed — NII should benefit from higher-for-longer. Bulls argue JPM will raise or reaffirm the $104.5B guide; bears worry about deposit repricing costs and loan growth softening as corporates defer capex in a tariff environment.

- **IB fees: January window vs. March paralysis:** JPM guided IB fees +mid-to-high teens YoY going into Q1. January saw strong deal activity; February and March froze as tariff escalation and Iran war disrupted CEO confidence. Dimon himself said "boardrooms are on pause." Advisory fees could disappoint the guide while DCM/bond underwriting held up better given rate-driven refinancing demand. Consensus IB fee line: ~$2.2–2.4B (vs. ~$2.0B in Q1 2025). JPM's Q1 2026 Company Update also reiterated a positive pipeline, particularly in CIB.

- **Trading revenues — can the CIB deliver?** JPM's COO Jennifer Piepszak guided "low double digit" trading revenue growth in mid-February, when the bar was ~$8–9B. With FICC boosted by Iran/rate volatility and equities driven by massive portfolio rotation, the actual figure could be meaningfully above guidance. The Feb 23 NII raise was explicitly attributed to Markets — providing further signaling that Q1 trading was running ahead of plan even before the March/April spike in volatility. FICC consensus: ~$5.5–6.0B; equities ~$3.8–4.2B.

- **Apple Card portfolio integration:** JPM completed the Apple Card portfolio acquisition in Q1 2026, adding high-yielding revolving credit card balances. Internal Chase card data from early 2026 shows discretionary spending up ~2.6% YTD — consumer is not "broken." However, the NCO rate on cards is expected to run at ~3.4% in 2026 (per the Feb 23 8-K), up from the "free money" era. Any incremental deterioration above 3.4% would be a negative signal.

- **Credit quality — is the consumer credit cycle turning?** JPM saw modest reserve releases in recent quarters, but net charge-offs in consumer cards have been normalizing toward ~3.4%. With Dimon citing 50–50 recession odds, the buy-side watches provision for credit losses closely. Strong NFP (178K) and 4.3% UE reduces near-term tail risk, but the ceasefire is fragile and macro uncertainty remains. Any incremental reserve build above $3.5B would read as a signal that JPM's credit team sees deteriorating conditions.

- **Expenses: $105B guide for FY2026 — can Q1 stay below $27B?** Expense discipline (or lack thereof) has been the persistent JPM bear thesis. In Q4 2025, expenses came in high. Q1 compensation accruals (bonus accruals based on strong trading) could push expenses toward the upper end. Efficiency ratio watch: JPM targets <60% for 2026. The Feb 23 update reaffirmed the $105B expense guide, including ~$19.8B in technology spend (+10% YoY) reflecting heavy AI investment.

## What Matters This Print

- **EPS:** $5.43 consensus (Yahoo Finance/prediction market as of Mar 30); Barchart range $5.32–$5.74. Q1 2025 actual was $5.07. The ~7% YoY growth bar is achievable if trading is strong and credit holds.
- **Net Revenue:** $48.61B consensus. Q4 2025 was $45.80B; Q1 2025 was $45.33B. The Q1 step-up is meaningful and trading-driven.
- **Net Interest Income (NII):** Q1 quarterly NII implied ~$24–25B at the $104.5B annual run-rate. **Watch for any upward revision above $104.5B** — that would be the most important NII signal given higher-for-longer. The February raise to $104.5B was the last official guidance.
- **CIB Trading Revenue (FICC + Equities):** Total trading consensus ~$9.5–10.5B. FICC ~$5.5–6.0B; Equities ~$3.8–4.2B. Any combined print above $10.5B would exceed even the "low double digit" growth guide and drive significant upside.
- **Investment Banking Fees (Advisory + ECM + DCM):** Consensus ~$2.2–2.4B. Q1 2025 was ~$2.0B. The mid-to-high teens growth guide implies ~$2.3–2.4B.
- **Provision for Credit Losses:** Consensus ~$3.2–3.5B. Q4 2025 was ~$3.3B. Card NCO rate guidance of ~3.4% is the benchmark; any acceleration is a downside signal. Strong NFP (178K, 4.3% UE) is a tailwind for credit quality.
- **CET1 Ratio:** ~15.5% expected (Q4 2025 was 14.6% per the 8-K). JPM has been building capital; watch for any commentary on accelerating buybacks or dividend increases.
- **ROTCE:** JPM targets 17% through-the-cycle; Q1 should track at ~19–20% if trading outperforms.
- **Dimon commentary:** Tone on recession probability, tariff impact on corporate capex, and ceasefire/geopolitical outlook will be the most-watched qualitative signal in the sector. Any update to 50–50 recession odds (up or down) will move markets.
