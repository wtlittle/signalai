## 1. Set-Up

**Current price:** $200.47 | **52-week range:** $188.16–$329.93 | **Market cap:** $81.1B

ADP has shed roughly 39% from its 52-week high, trading just above its 52-week low of $188.16. The stock is one of the worst-performing mega-cap software/services names over the trailing 12 months, underperforming both the S&P 500 and HCM peers. The sell-off has been driven by three converging pressures: (1) structural concern that AI-driven headcount reduction will compress the "pays-per-control" revenue base; (2) employment data deceleration — the BLS reported February 2026 payrolls fell 92,000 with unemployment climbing to 4.4% before a partial recovery to +178,000 and 4.3% in March; and (3) multiple contraction from a peak ~30x P/E to ~19x on trailing earnings.

**Valuation vs. peers:** ADP trades at ~19x trailing P/E (TTM EPS ~$10.40 per quote data) versus its 5-year average of ~29x. On a forward basis, using FY2026 consensus of ~$12.25 blended annual EPS, the stock sits at ~16.4x forward — the lowest in over a decade. Paychex (PAYX) trades at approximately 22x forward, while cloud-native Workday (WDAY) commands 25x+ on NTM earnings. The ADP discount reflects the pays-per-control uncertainty, but the absolute level is historically compressed.

**Key context from Q2 FY2026 (reported Jan. 28):** Revenue grew 6% YoY to $5.36B (beat by ~$22M). Adjusted EPS of $2.62 beat the $2.58 estimate. ## 2. Key Debates and Variants

**Debate 1 — AI as structural headcount suppressant or as ADP tailwind?**
The bear case (Wells Fargo Underweight, JPMorgan Underweight, Jefferies Underperform) argues that agentic AI will systematically reduce the number of human workers per company — the unit upon which ADP's pays-per-control economics rest. The bull case (Guggenheim, Cantor Fitzgerald) counters that AI will *increase* HCM platform complexity and compliance requirements, making ADP's integrated suite more, not less, valuable. ADP's own "ADP Assist" agentic AI suite (payroll agents, HR agents, tax registration agents) is live and scaling, suggesting the company intends to participate in the efficiency wave rather than simply absorb it. Consensus is arguably too pessimistic here if ADP can successfully monetize AI features through ARPU uplift rather than watching headcount decline.

**Debate 2 — Employment resilience and the March rebound read-through**
The March 2026 payroll print (+178,000) rebounded sharply from February's -92,000 shock. For Q3 (quarter ending March 31), ADP's pays-per-control metric will primarily reflect January–March employment conditions. The February softness likely weighed on pays-per-control. The critical question is whether management guides for pays-per-control to remain "approximately flat" for the full year or signals further deceleration heading into FY2027 — the latter would reset forward estimates materially.

**Debate 3 — Client retention inflection**
Q2 FY2026 retention declined modestly vs. prior year; management maintained a 10–30 bps full-year decline forecast. Lyric's pipeline expansion (>70% new logos) and the WorkForce Software integration win for the 75,000-employee European bank suggest competitive positioning is improving even as the macro clouds retention metrics. An upside surprise on retention (narrowing the guided decline) would be a significant positive data point.

**Where consensus may be wrong:** Street models are underweighting the client funds float tailwind. With average funds balance growth raised to 4–5% at a ~3.4% yield, client funds interest revenue is guided to $1.31–1.33B for FY2026 — a near-pure-margin revenue stream that contributes disproportionately to EPS. If short-term rates remain sticky, this line could continue to exceed expectations.
