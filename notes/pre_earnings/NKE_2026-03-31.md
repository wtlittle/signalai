# NKE Pre-Earnings: Q3 FY2026 (Reports March 31, 2026)

**Prepared:** March 22, 2026 | **Reports:** March 31, 2026, ~1:15 PM PT | **Horizon:** 1–3 years

---

## Set-up

NKE closed Friday, March 20 at $52.37, touching a fresh 52-week low of $52.17 — down approximately 18% YTD and 35% from its 52-week high of $80.17. The stock has shed roughly 70% of its value from the pandemic-era peak, and investor patience is thinning as the turnaround under CEO Elliott Hill enters its second year. Market cap stands at ~$77.4B. On trailing earnings, the stock trades at 30.6x P/E — optically expensive given compressed profitability — and roughly 20x EV/EBITDA on FY2025 numbers. Forward multiples, however, look more compelling if FY2027 normalization materializes: consensus projects EPS of roughly $3.10–$3.30 for FY2027, implying ~16–17x forward P/E at current prices. EV/Revenue sits near 1.7x, below the 2.4–3.1x range Nike commanded between FY2014 and FY2023. The market is not paying for recovery; it is pricing in prolonged execution risk.

Sell-side consensus is a soft Buy: 12 of 17 tracked analysts are bullish, 5 are neutral, and none carry Sell ratings. Average price target of $72.82 (range $58–$90) implies ~39% upside from current levels, but that spread itself signals wide uncertainty. Barclays upgraded to Overweight in March with a $73 target, citing inventory normalization and Running momentum. UBS cut its target to $58 (Neutral) one day before earnings, flagging the risk of disappointing forward guidance. BTIG maintains a $90 Buy. Short interest is modest at ~2.9% of float (42.4M shares as of February 27, 2026) — the market is not heavily positioned short, suggesting limited squeeze potential but also muted structural tailwind from cover-buying on a beat.

What is the market pricing in? The stock's trajectory implies the consensus scenario is one of continued margin pressure through fiscal year-end, a China business that remains a drag through at least mid-FY2027, and a recovery that does not begin to read through meaningfully in earnings until H2 FY2027 at the earliest. The Q2 FY2026 quarter (reported December 18, 2025) beat on revenue ($12.43B vs. $12.21B est.) and EPS ($0.53 vs. $0.37 est.), yet the stock fell 10% on the day as management guided Q3 revenue "down low single digits" and acknowledged China as a "longer road." That reaction — a massive EPS beat punished with a 10% selloff — encapsulates the positioning: buy-siders are not chasing beats, they are waiting for evidence the trough is in. Q3 FY2026 is the next test.

## Key Debates and Variants

- **CEO Elliott Hill's turnaround pace and the "Win Now" playbook.** Hill, who took the helm in October 2024, has reorganized the leadership structure (all geographies now report directly to him), launched the "Sport Offense" framework, and is unwinding the prior regime's DTC-first over-pivot. Q2 demonstrated real progress: North America wholesale +24%, Running +20% for the second consecutive quarter, and Nike.com's best Black Friday ever. The bear case is that the easy wins (liquidating aging Classics inventory, re-engaging lapsed wholesale partners) are now largely captured in North America, and the harder work — rebuilding China, fixing EMEA's EBIT margins, relaunching Converse — remains ahead. Bulls argue the North America playbook is exportable; bears see idiosyncratic complexity in each remaining geography.

- **Wholesale channel recovery vs. DTC margin dilution.** The strategic pivot back to wholesale — the channel grew 8% in Q2 vs. Nike Direct's -9% — is volume-positive but structurally margin-dilutive. Wholesale as a share of revenue has risen back toward 62%. Management targets a hybrid model but has not articulated a clear endpoint for the DTC-to-wholesale rebalancing. The key question: at what wholesale/DTC mix does gross margin stabilize? Every 100bps of mix shift from DTC to wholesale costs approximately 150–200bps of gross margin, all else equal. Bulls argue the volume leverage and inventory health gains from wholesale outweigh the mix penalty; bears note that Nike's long-term margin thesis was always predicated on higher DTC penetration.

- **Greater China: cyclical trough or structural impairment?** Greater China revenue fell 16% YoY in Q2 FY2026 (the sixth consecutive quarterly decline), with EBIT down 49% and Nike Digital down 36%. Management acknowledged in December that inventory obsolescence charges in China "were not contemplated 90 days ago." Competition from domestic brands Anta and Li-Ning, a consumer preference shift toward value, and Nike's own missteps in pricing and promotional discipline have created a compounding cycle: off-price selling → erodes brand premium → accelerates shift to competitors → forces more promotion. Nike's initial pilot of a revamped mono-brand store in Beijing and Shanghai showed encouraging comp sales, but scale-up is measured in years, not quarters. RBC Capital (Outperform, $78) argues no fundamental impediment to a return to China growth within 1–2 years; BNP Paribas (Underperform, $35) cites Pou Sheng's profit warning as evidence that channel partner distress will extend the pain.

- **Tariff headwinds and gross margin recovery timeline.** CFO Matt Friend confirmed on the Q2 call that U.S. tariffs on Southeast Asian manufacturing (primarily Vietnam and Indonesia) represent $1.5B in annualized incremental product costs — a gross headwind of approximately 320bps to FY2026 gross margin. Nike has taken actions to reduce this to a net ~120bps through price adjustments, sourcing shifts, and operational efficiencies. For Q3, management guided gross margin down 175–225bps YoY, implying approximately 39.2–39.7% (vs. 41.5% in Q3 FY2025). Excluding tariffs, gross margin would actually expand positively — a key bull argument. The core debate is how quickly Nike can diversify its manufacturing base away from high-tariff jurisdictions: management says "beginning to take actions" but offers no firm timeline on sourcing reallocation at scale.

- **Competitive dynamics: On Holding, Hoka, and the running revolution.** Nike lost meaningful share in performance running over 2022–2025 to On Holding (ONON) and Hoka (DECK) while focused on DTC and Classics-heavy repositioning. On is now a $3B+ revenue brand growing at 30%+ with 65% gross margins; Hoka is growing at ~20% annually. Nike's running resurgence — Structure 26 stability platform, 20%+ Running growth for two consecutive quarters — is a genuine positive signal but the structural shift in consumer preference toward technically differentiated "super shoes" is a longer-term brand-building challenge Nike cannot solve in one product cycle. The NFL, NBA, and World Cup football booking (order books ~40% above 2022 levels) provide a different growth vector, but the performance running brand gap with challengers remains a watch item.

### 1. Revenue and Channel Growth

Management guided Q3 revenue "down low single digits" on a reported basis with a ~3-point FX tailwind. Currency-neutral, this implies -3% to -4% ex-FX. The comp is relatively benign vs. Q3 FY2025's $11.27B. Watch for any commentary on order book trends heading into summer/World Cup season — a building order book would be a leading indicator for FY2027 re-acceleration.

### 2. Gross Margin Trajectory

| | Q3 FY2025 Actual | Mgmt Guidance Q3 FY2026 | Bullish Signal | Bearish Signal |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Gross Margin | 41.5% | **39.2–39.7% (down 175–225bps)** | At the better end (~39.7%) or above; tariff mitigation visible | Worse than -225bps; China inventory charges surprise again |

The stated tariff headwind for Q3 is ~315bps. If Nike delivers within the guided range, it implies underlying (ex-tariff) gross margin expansion — the first time this cycle. That would be a materially bullish signal for FY2027 margin recovery. A miss below guidance (driven by China obsolescence charges or incremental DTC deleverage) would be the largest negative surprise, potentially driving the stock toward the low-to-mid $40s. Key: Did the Q3 FY2026 $300M restructuring charge (filed in SEC disclosure March 5, primarily severance) flow through COGS or SG&A? Watch the line items carefully.

### 3. Greater China Revenue Trends

| | Q3 FY2025 Actual | Implied Q3 FY2026 | Bullish Signal | Bearish Signal |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Greater China Revenue | $1,512M | **~$1,250–1,350M (−11% to −17% YoY)** | Decline moderates to -10% or less; comp sales inflect | -20%+ decline; partner inventory charges repeat |
| Greater China EBIT | $377M | Significant YoY decline expected | EBIT decline narrows; store pilot scales to 10+ doors | Another surprise obsolescence charge; EBIT -50%+ again |

China is the single most watched number. Management said "performance similar to Q2" for China — which implies ~−16% is consensus. A sequential improvement in the rate of decline (e.g., −10% to −12%) would be received as a major positive because it would indicate the inventory cycle is cresting. Running reportedly continued to grow in China in Q2, which is one nascent positive signal. The Pou Sheng profit warning in February suggests wholesale conditions in China remain stressed.

### 4. Inventory Levels and Turns

| | Q2 FY2025 | Q3 FY2025 | Q2 FY2026 | Q3 FY2026 Target |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Inventory ($B) | $7.98B | $7.54B | **$7.73B** | Below $7.5B (YoY decline) |
| YoY Change | +3.0% | +2.7% | −3.2% | −3 to −5% expected |

Inventory peaked at $8.25B in Q1 FY2025 and has been declining gradually. At $7.73B in Q2, North America and EMEA are described as "healthy and clean." The problem remains Greater China (high aged inventory, elevated returns) and pockets of APLA. A sequential inventory decline in Q3 — especially if accompanied by a lower mix of closeout units — would confirm the cleaning cycle is intact. An uptick in inventory or a surprise in closeout-to-markdown charges would suggest the China clean-up is not tracking to plan.

Guidance may be more important than the print itself. The market needs to hear: (1) that the Classics decline is nearing its floor (management has said >$4B in Classics revenue will be stripped by fiscal year-end), (2) that the North America wholesale order book is building into Summer/Fall 2026, and (3) any signal on the China trajectory in H2 FY2026. An absence of explicit FY2027 guidance is normal, but tone matters — listen for language about "return to growth" in the back half of FY2027 vs. more hedged commentary on "continued investments" and "multiple headwinds."

## Scenario Grid

### Base Case (60% probability)

**What happens:** Revenue comes in at ~$11.1–11.3B, roughly flat YoY, in line with consensus. Gross margin lands at approximately 39.5–39.7%, at the better end of guidance. North America delivers modest wholesale growth; China decline continues at approximately -14% to -16% YoY but shows no new surprises (no incremental obsolescence charges). Inventory ticks down modestly YoY. EPS is approximately $0.28–0.35, consistent with the ~$0.31 consensus estimate. Management guides Q4 revenues approximately flat to slightly down, with gross margin described as "sequentially improving." Tone is measured; Hill reiterates "middle innings" language but points to World Cup bookings and North America momentum as tangible forward proof points. No new structural guidance for FY2027.

**Stock reaction:** Flat to ±3%. The stock may see a mild relief rally given the recent selloff to 52-week lows, but a lack of positive inflection in China and no upside to guidance would limit upside. Likely trading range: $51–$57.

---

### Bull Case (20% probability)

**What happens:** Revenue beats at $11.4–11.6B driven by North America wholesale outperformance and EMEA stabilization. Gross margin comes in at 40%+, above the guided range — either because tariff mitigation is tracking ahead of plan or because China inventory charges are materially lower than feared. Greater China decline moderates to approximately -10%, with management citing improving sell-through in the Beijing/Shanghai store pilots and lower promotional activity. Inventory falls below $7.5B with the closeout mix improving. Management provides constructive language on H2 FY2026 and explicitly frames FY2027 as a year of margin recovery, pointing to the Classics headwind cycling off and the World Cup demand beginning to pull through. EBIT margin path toward double digits becomes credible in the near term.

**Stock reaction:** +8% to +12%. A China inflection and gross margin beat would be the most powerful catalyst combination this cycle. Price range: $56–$59. Longer-dated, this scenario sets up a move toward the mid-$60s over 12 months.

---

### Bear Case (20% probability)

**What happens:** Revenue misses at $10.8–11.0B, driven by weaker-than-expected North America wholesale (liquidation activity from prior quarters fades and new product doesn't fully replace it), a worse-than-guided China decline (-20%+), and additional currency headwinds in EMEA. Gross margin misses the guided range, landing at 38.5–39.0%, due to incremental China obsolescence charges — a repeat of the Q2 surprise. Management guides Q4 revenues "down low to mid single digits" and declines to provide constructive FY2027 commentary, citing macro uncertainty and tariff risk. The $300M restructuring charge flows through in a way that obscures normalized profitability. EPS comes in at $0.20 or below. Any mention of reviewing Converse's future (potential divestiture) would add another overhang.

**Stock reaction:** -10% to -15%. A China re-acceleration of inventory charges after management said conditions were "similar to Q2" would be an act of guidance credibility destruction. Stock could re-test low-$40s, returning to sub-2016 price levels and putting the dividend (currently yielding ~2.5%) under scrutiny. Price range: $44–$47. In this scenario, institutional holders would likely extend underweights rather than accumulate.

## Key Watch Items on the Call

1. **China tone and pilot store data:** How many mono-brand pilot stores have been converted? What are comp sales trends vs. the broader fleet?
2. **Gross margin components:** How much of any beat/miss is tariff-driven vs. mix-driven vs. obsolescence-driven? The ex-tariff gross margin is the cleanest signal of underlying brand health.
3. **World Cup order book:** Management said football bookings are 40% above 2022 levels — any update here would help quantify the FY2027 revenue tailwind.
4. **SG&A and demand creation:** Up "low single digits" in Q3 per guidance. Are marketing investments beginning to read through in brand metrics (consideration, NPS, social engagement)?
5. **Converse restructuring scope:** Any language on the future of Converse (the brand is down ~20% YoY and burning negative EBIT) — divestiture language would be a negative overhang even if strategically rational.
6. **Buyback activity:** Nike has been buying back stock. Any update on capital return priorities at current prices would signal management confidence.

The stock is not cheap on trailing multiples given suppressed earnings, but it screens as historically inexpensive on a revenue and normalized earnings basis.
