# Wells Fargo & Company (WFC) -- Pre-Earnings Note
**Earnings Date:** April 14, 2026 (BMO) | **Quarter:** Q1 FY2026 | **Days Until Report:** 9

---

## Set-up

WFC closed at $80.60 on Apr 2, down approximately **15.3% YTD** from its Jan 2 close of $95.20 — the worst YTD performer among the six large-cap banks. The 52-week range is $58.42–$97.76; WFC hit its 52-week high in early January before a sharp selloff accelerating through February and March that took the stock to $74 mid-March before a partial recovery to $80. P/E of ~12.9x trailing EPS ($6.26 TTM). Market cap: ~$249B.

The underperformance is rooted in WFC's specific vulnerabilities: (1) heavy NII sensitivity — WFC is the most NII-levered of the large banks (>55% of revenues), making it uniquely exposed to the "higher-for-longer is good" vs. "deflation risk / recession / curve flattening is bad" debate; (2) the asset cap — the Fed's $1.95T asset cap (a legacy of the 2016 fake accounts scandal) constrains WFC's ability to grow its balance sheet, preventing it from fully monetizing the rate environment; (3) tariff-driven economic slowdown hits WFC's consumer and commercial loan book more directly given its domestic orientation; (4) Q4 2025 was a disappointment — revenues of $21.29B missed the $21.65B consensus, EPS beat at $1.76 vs. $1.66, but the stock fell 4.6% as NII guidance for Q1 2026 implied a modest step-down before recovery.

WFC CEO Charlie Scharf has guided for a lift from the eventual asset cap removal — widely expected in 2026 if regulatory progress continues — but this is a binary catalyst that markets continue to discount unevenly. In Q1 commentary (per The Financial Brand), Scharf noted: "We have not seen an impact on their condition yet" regarding tariffs on corporate clients, but acknowledged a "transitory environment" of uncertainty.

The macro context — Iran war, rates elevated but volatile, deflation risk, consumer resilience being tested — is directly relevant: WFC's consumer and small business book has more lower-income exposure than JPM or BAC, and rising energy/food prices from Iran-driven inflation create headwinds for those segments. Conversely, WFC's mortgage franchise and rate-sensitive businesses benefit from a steeper yield curve.

## Key Debates and Variants

- **NII trajectory and asset cap timing:** WFC guided for modest NII pressure in early 2026 (Q4 2025 NII was $11.84B) before a recovery through the year as rate mix improves and the asset cap is eventually lifted. Q1 2026 consensus NII is ~$11.6-11.9B — essentially flat. The market debate is whether WFC will guide to a full-year NII number that accelerates in H2 2026 (the bull case) or whether the higher-for-longer environment creates deposit cost pressure that offsets yield benefits. The $95B+ NII figure cited for WFC's medium-term target is key context.

- **Asset cap removal: when and what's the EPS uplift?** The most binary WFC catalyst. Scharf has indicated regulatory dialogue is productive; some analysts expect formal cap removal in Q2-Q3 2026, which would allow WFC to accelerate loan growth and balance sheet expansion. The cap removal could add $2-3B in annual NII over 2-3 years. Any regulatory update or management signaling at this print would be significant. A positive signal here is the #1 near-term catalyst to close WFC's valuation gap.

- **Credit quality in consumer and commercial banking:** WFC's loan book is more domestically exposed and consumer-heavy than peers. Rising delinquencies in cards and auto (visible across the consumer lending space) are a concern. WFC's Q4 2025 showed some normalization; Q1 2026 in a tariff/stagflation environment could push net charge-offs higher. Watch provision for credit losses vs. the Q4 2025 baseline of ~$1.0B.

- **Non-interest income diversification:** WFC's IB ambitions remain modest vs. peers (WFC's IB fees are <$0.5B/quarter). Fee income diversification away from NII is critical to the re-rating story. Watch wealth management, card fees, and mortgage banking (where higher rates are a headwind for origination but WFC's servicing book benefits from elevated rates).

- **Expense discipline — efficiency ratio target of ~62%:** WFC has been in an efficiency optimization drive. Q4 2025 non-interest expense was ~$13.9B. Q1 2026 consensus expects ~$13.5-14.0B. Any expense surprise upward would pressure the already-thin margin for error given the NII headwind.

- **Tariff/consumer credit stress:** WFC has the highest relative exposure to the "main street" borrower among large caps. Early signals of consumer stress (lower-income segment delinquency normalization, card credit normalization) are being watched closely. Scharf's commentary on credit line utilization and card performance is a key qualitative data point.

## What Matters This Print

- **EPS:** $1.57 consensus (range: $1.45–$1.75). Q1 2025 actual was $1.27. The +24% YoY growth bar reflects primarily: (1) lapping Q1 2025's low baseline; (2) NII stabilization; (3) expense efficiency gains.
- **Net Revenue:** $21.80B consensus. Q4 2025 actual was $21.29B (light miss). Q1 2025 was $20.15B.
- **Net Interest Income (NII):** Consensus ~$11.7-12.0B quarterly. Q4 2025 was $11.84B. Any NII guidance for FY2026 (consensus ~$87-88B annual, implying recovery into H2) is the key metric — a raise would be bullish.
- **Non-Interest Income:** Consensus ~$8.5-9.0B quarterly. Watch for IB fees, card fees, and mortgage banking.
- **Provision for Credit Losses:** Consensus ~$1.0-1.3B. Q4 2025 was ~$1.0B. Watch for any reserve build — this is the most critical downside risk for WFC given its consumer exposure in a tariff/stagflation environment.
- **Efficiency Ratio:** Target <62%; watch Q1 run-rate.
- **CET1 Ratio:** ~11.0-11.5% expected. WFC has been rebuilding capital; any buyback acceleration is a positive signal.
- **Asset Cap Update:** Any regulatory language about cap removal timeline is the most important non-financial disclosure of the quarter.
