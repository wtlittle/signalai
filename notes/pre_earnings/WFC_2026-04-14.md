# Wells Fargo & Company (WFC) -- Pre-Earnings Note
**Earnings Date:** April 14, 2026 (BMO) | **Quarter:** Q1 FY2026 | **Days Until Report:** 7

## Set-up

WFC closed at $80.60 on Apr 2, down approximately **15.3% YTD** from its Jan 2 close of $95.20 — the worst YTD performer among the six large-cap banks. The 52-week range is $58.42–$97.76; WFC hit its 52-week high in early January before a sharp selloff that took the stock to $74 mid-March before a partial recovery. The stock was trading near $79.61 in late March (per Sahm Capital / SimplyWallSt). P/E of ~12.9x trailing EPS ($6.26 TTM). Market cap: ~$249B.

**Monday Apr 7 open update:** Pakistan's army chief brokered an overnight framework — the "Islamabad Accord" — proposing a 45-day ceasefire between the US and Iran, with Strait of Hormuz reopening in phase one (per Axios/Reuters/Al Jazeera, Apr 5–6). **Brent has pulled back to $108.42** (from ~$115), and **VIX is 24.88** (from 27.61 on Apr 2). S&P 500 opened +0.3% on ceasefire hopes. Ceasefire talks remain fragile — Iran has declined to reopen Hormuz under a "temporary" deal. **March CPI is due Friday April 11** — the key macro catalyst of the week. **NFP 178K / unemployment 4.3%** — strong labor market, broadly positive for consumer and commercial credit quality, a particularly important signal for WFC given its consumer-heavy domestic loan book.

The underperformance is rooted in WFC's specific vulnerabilities: (1) heavy NII sensitivity — WFC is the most NII-levered of the large banks (>55% of revenues), making it uniquely exposed to rate debates; (2) the asset cap — the Fed's $1.95T asset cap constrains WFC's ability to grow its balance sheet; (3) tariff-driven economic slowdown hits WFC's consumer and commercial loan book more directly given its domestic orientation; (4) Q4 2025 was a disappointment — revenues of $21.29B missed the $21.65B consensus, EPS beat at $1.76 vs. $1.66, but the stock fell 4.6% as NII guidance for Q1 2026 implied a modest step-down before recovery.

**Analyst activity since prior note:**

McDonald cited a more subdued NII outlook, aligning projections with WFC's own guidance of ~$50B in NII for FY2026. Truist lowered buyback expectations to **$3.5B/quarter for FY2026** (from higher prior estimates) and $4.0B/quarter for FY2027. EPS forecasts retained at **$6.85 FY2026** and **$7.x FY2027**.
- **Barchart/Wall Street consensus (Mar 25):** EPS consensus **$1.56/share** (+22.8% vs. $1.27 Q1 2025 actual). Mean analyst PT: **$101.42** (27.4% upside from ~$79.61 close), with 14 Strong Buy, 4 Moderate Buy, 9 Hold among 27 analysts — a "Moderate Buy" overall.
- **Sahm Capital / SimplyWallSt (Mar 25):** At least one major brokerage (likely Truist) trimmed NII and buyback assumptions; fair value debate ongoing ($74.70 bear DCF vs. $128.41 bull DCF).

WFC CEO Charlie Scharf noted in Q1 commentary: "We have not seen an impact on their condition yet" regarding tariffs on corporate clients, but acknowledged a "transitory environment" of uncertainty.

## Key Debates and Variants

- **NII trajectory and asset cap timing:** WFC guided for modest NII pressure in early 2026 (Q4 2025 NII was $11.84B) before a recovery through the year as rate mix improves and the asset cap is eventually lifted. **Truist has revised its NII projection to align with WFC's own ~$50B FY2026 NII guide** (implying ~$12.5B/quarter average). Q1 2026 consensus NII is ~$11.6–11.9B — essentially flat vs. Q4. The market debate is whether WFC will guide to a full-year NII number that accelerates in H2 2026 (the bull case) or whether deposit cost pressure offsets yield benefits. Higher-for-longer (if CPI remains elevated) is net positive for WFC NII trajectory — the strong NFP report and pending CPI data are key inputs.

- **Asset cap removal: when and what's the EPS uplift?** The most binary WFC catalyst. Scharf has indicated productive regulatory dialogue; some analysts expect formal cap removal in Q2–Q3 2026, which would allow WFC to accelerate loan growth. The cap removal could add $2–3B in annual NII over 2–3 years. Truist's model trims were explicitly tied to pushing back the timeline for the cap-removal benefit. Any regulatory update or management signaling at this print is the #1 near-term re-rating catalyst.

- **Buybacks: Truist cut to $3.5B/quarter for FY2026:** Truist's reduction in buyback expectations (to $3.5B/quarter from higher prior estimates) reflects more conservative capital return assumptions pending: (1) asset cap removal timeline uncertainty; (2) NII recovery timing. Watch the actual Q1 buyback figures and any guidance on the pace for 2026 — confirmation of $3.5B/quarter would be broadly in-line; any surprise above $4B would be bullish.

- **Credit quality in consumer and commercial banking:** WFC's loan book is more domestically exposed and consumer-heavy than peers. Rising delinquencies in cards and auto have been normalizing across the consumer lending space. Strong NFP (178K) and 4.3% UE is the most important positive data point here — a strong labor market is the primary backstop for WFC's consumer credit quality. Watch provision for credit losses vs. the Q4 2025 baseline of ~$1.0B.

- **Non-interest income diversification:** WFC's IB ambitions remain modest vs. peers (IB fees <$0.5B/quarter). Fee income diversification away from NII is critical to the re-rating story. Watch wealth management, card fees, and mortgage banking (where higher rates are a headwind for origination but WFC's servicing book benefits from elevated rates).

- **Expense discipline — efficiency ratio target of ~62%:** WFC has been in an efficiency optimization drive. Q4 2025 non-interest expense was ~$13.9B. Q1 2026 consensus expects ~$13.5–14.0B. Any expense surprise upward would pressure the already-thin NII margin.

- **Tariff/consumer credit stress and the "main street" borrower:** WFC has the highest relative exposure to the "main street" borrower among large caps. Early signals of consumer stress (lower-income segment delinquency normalization, card credit normalization) are being watched closely. Scharf's commentary on credit line utilization and card performance is a key qualitative data point. The strong NFP report mitigates but does not eliminate this concern.

## What Matters This Print

- **EPS:** $1.56–1.57 consensus (Barchart $1.56, Public $1.57–1.58; range ~$1.45–$1.75). Q1 2025 actual was $1.27. The +22.8% YoY growth bar reflects primarily: (1) lapping Q1 2025's low baseline; (2) NII stabilization; (3) expense efficiency gains. WFC has beaten in each of the last 4 quarters; Q4 2025 beat was +6%.
- **Net Revenue:** ~$21.80B consensus. Q4 2025 actual was $21.29B (light miss). Q1 2025 was $20.15B.
- **Net Interest Income (NII):** Consensus ~$11.7–12.0B quarterly. Q4 2025 was $11.84B. **Watch for FY2026 NII guidance** (WFC's own guide ~$50B annual; Truist aligns to this). A raise to $50.5–51B would be bullish; a cut below $49B would be bearish.
- **Non-Interest Income:** Consensus ~$8.5–9.0B quarterly. Watch for IB fees, card fees, and mortgage banking.
- **Provision for Credit Losses:** Consensus ~$1.0–1.3B. Q4 2025 was ~$1.0B. Strong NFP (178K, 4.3% UE) supports credit stability; any build above $1.5B would be a bearish signal.
- **Efficiency Ratio:** Target <62%; watch Q1 run-rate.
- **CET1 Ratio:** ~11.0–11.5% expected. WFC has been rebuilding capital; watch buyback pace — Truist models $3.5B/quarter.
- **Asset Cap Update:** Any regulatory language about cap removal timeline is the most important non-financial disclosure of the quarter. This remains the single most binary catalyst for WFC.
