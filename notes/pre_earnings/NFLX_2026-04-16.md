## Key Debates and Variants

1. **Advertising revenue trajectory and CPM pressure**: Netflix's ad tier launched in 2022 and has been scaling; consensus expects ~$3B ad revenue in 2026 (~6% of total). Q1 is seasonally soft for advertising. Given the current macro backdrop (Deflation Risk, tariff uncertainty suppressing corporate budgets), CPMs could face headwinds. The street is watching for any color on ad-supported MAU penetration, CPM trends, and advertiser renewal rates. If ad revenue is tracking above consensus ($750M in Q1), it's a positive catalyst for re-rating.

2. **Pricing power and ARPU growth without subscriber disclosure**: Netflix raised prices across all subscription tiers by $1-$2, most recently effective for UCAN. Oppenheimer's March PT raise was specifically driven by this pricing action. The key debate: does higher ARPU translate to near-term revenue upside, or does price sensitivity at the margin lead to elevated churn in lower-income cohorts? Since Netflix no longer reports quarterly subs, ARPU x implied subscribers is the only way to triangulate.

3. **Content slate and engagement durability**: Netflix is spending $20B on content in 2026. Q1 2026 slate includes continuation of major original franchises. The question is whether password-sharing enforcement benefits (which drove the H2 2023-2024 subscriber surge) are now fully absorbed, and what the "next lever" for engagement-driven growth looks like. Live sports (NFL) and gaming are experimental but not yet material.

4. **Operating margin discipline vs. content acceleration**: Management guided 31.5% operating margin for FY2026 -- that is $3.8-3.9B on ~$12.2B Q1 revenue. If content costs are tracking above plan or marketing spend is elevated, Q1 margin could print below 30%. Conversely, if the price increase sticks with minimal churn, revenue upside could flow directly to margin.

5. **Macro and engagement sensitivity in Deflation Risk**: In recessions, Netflix has historically been "recession-resistant" -- subscribers stay subscribed. But in a trade-war/deflation environment with consumer confidence declining, the risk is not churn but rather: (a) potential US household budget cuts, (b) FX headwinds on international revenue, and (c) advertiser pullback. International revenue is ~50% of total and benefits from dollar weakening (but could hurt if EM currencies weaken vs. USD).

6. **Competitive dynamics and Disney+/Amazon dynamics**: Disney+ is executing on a bundle strategy (Disney+/Hulu/ESPN+) that could pressure Netflix at the margin in the US. Amazon Prime Video added ads by default in Jan 2024 and is scaling its ad business aggressively. Netflix's differentiation is content quality, not price -- but content leadership requires sustained $20B+ investment levels.

## What Matters This Print

1. **EPS**: Consensus $0.76-$0.786 (up 15.2% from $0.66 in Q1 2025). Q1 2025 was a strong beat ($0.66 actual vs. $0.57 est, +15.8%). The bar is higher this year -- a similar magnitude beat would imply ~$0.87-0.88 EPS, which would be a major positive surprise.

2. **Revenue**: Consensus $12.17B. Q1 2025 actual: $10.54B. Implies +15.4% YoY growth. Q4 2025 was $12.05B (+$81M beat). Street will want acceleration, not deceleration.

3. **Operating Margin**: Guided ~31.5% for FY2026. Q1 2026 operating income consensus implies ~$3.9B on $12.17B revenue (~32% margin). If print is above 33%, it's a clear upside signal.

4. **Q2 2026 Revenue Guidance**: Netflix guides one quarter ahead. Q1 2025 actuals were $10.54B; Q2 2025 actuals were $11.08B. Q2 2026 consensus is $12.57B (+13.5% YoY). Any guidance above $13B would be a positive catalyst.

5. **Ad Tier Commentary**: Revenue contribution, MAU for ad-supported, CPM trends, advertiser demand in the current climate. This is the fastest-growing and highest-uncertainty revenue line. Any quantitative disclosure (ad revenue >$800M in Q1) would narrow the uncertainty premium.
