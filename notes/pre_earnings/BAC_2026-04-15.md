# Bank of America Corporation (BAC) -- Pre-Earnings Note
**Earnings Date:** April 15, 2026 (~6:45am ET) | **Quarter:** Q1 FY2026 | **Days Until Report:** 10

---

## Set-up

BAC closed at $49.38 on Apr 2, down approximately **11.7% YTD** from its Jan 2 close of $55.95. The 52-week range is $33.07–$57.55; the stock made a 52-week high in late December 2025/early January 2026 before a broad deterioration driven by macro anxiety. P/E of ~13.0x trailing EPS ($3.81 TTM). Market cap: ~$354B — the largest bank by market cap after JPM.

BAC has underperformed its large-cap bank peers YTD despite its strong macro narrative entering 2026. The underperformance reflects: (1) BAC's substantial duration risk and AFS securities portfolio — accumulated during the 2021-2022 low-rate period, the AOCI (accumulated other comprehensive income) losses remain a residual concern even as rising rates eventually help the reinvestment cycle; (2) heavy NII sensitivity with the "rate sensitivity" profile — BAC's NII benefits from rates staying higher, but has more deposit beta exposure than bears give it credit for; (3) greater relative exposure to consumer loan quality in a softening labor market.

BAC's Q4 2025 was broadly solid: revenues of $28.37B beat the $27.76B consensus (+$606M surprise), EPS of $0.98 beat the $0.96 estimate. But the stock fell 3.8% as investors focused on Moynihan's comments about IB pipelines ("healthy but waiting for clarity"), the softening consumer at the low-income end, and the continued drag from legacy AFS portfolio losses. Q1 2025 actual was $0.90 EPS on $27.37B revenues.

BAC reports at 6:45am ET on Apr 15 — the earliest of the six banks, before markets open and before MS. This means BAC's print will set the very first data point of the Apr 15 bank sector day and will influence MS's opening reaction.

The macro setup is specifically relevant for BAC: CEO Brian Moynihan highlighted in Q1 commentary that corporate clients are "sanguine on the current environment, but worried about their businesses" and that "decision path is slowing down." Moynihan noted IB pipeline is "healthy" but deal conversion is delayed pending tariff/regulatory clarity — a message that will resonate sharply given the April 2 Liberation Day tariff escalation (the last trading day before Easter). On the consumer side, Moynihan noted "aggregate consumer keeps pushing money into the economy" but spending patterns are shifting (away from travel/entertainment, toward essentials).

## Key Debates and Variants

- **NII trajectory and AOCI recovery:** BAC guided for 5-7% NII growth in FY2026 (following 7% growth in 2025). The higher-for-longer rate environment is net positive for BAC as its fixed-rate asset book matures and reinvests at higher yields. The key debate is whether deposit costs are stable or rising faster than yield gains. BAC's sensitivity table suggests ~$1.4B of annual NII benefit per 25bps of rate increase — with rates at 3.5-3.75% and no imminent cuts, this is a structural tailwind. The AOCI drag (~$85B accumulated over 2021-2022) is slowly amortizing; unrealized losses are improving but still weigh on tangible book value.

- **Global Markets trading vs. Merrill Lynch wealth dynamics:** BAC's Global Markets division (FICC + equities) is top-3 globally. Q2 2025 FICC was $3.2B (+16% YoY). With Iran war-driven rate and commodity volatility dominating Q1 2026, BAC's macro trading desks should have had excellent conditions. The equities business benefits from the same rotation dynamics (US tech to international/value) as peers. The Merrill Lynch wealth business is BAC's version of MS's Wealth Management — AUM-sensitive fee revenues that will have faced compression in Q1 from the equity market pullback, though net new client assets and financial advisor retention matter here.

- **IB fees: healthy pipeline, delayed conversion:** BAC's IB franchise ranks #3-4 globally. Moynihan was explicit that the IB pipeline is "healthy" but clients are waiting for clarity — a direct signal that Q1 IB fee conversion was below potential. Advisory fees are the primary drag; DCM should be solid (refinancing demand). The question is whether the pipeline stays intact into Q2 or whether prolonged tariff/geopolitical uncertainty causes deals to be canceled rather than merely delayed.

- **Consumer credit quality and the bifurcated consumer:** BAC CFO Alistair Borthwick has highlighted the bank's deliberate shift toward "secured lending and prime credit customers" — reducing unsecured consumer exposure. However, credit card is the main remaining consumer unsecured exposure. Moynihan explicitly flagged that lower-income consumers are showing "more stress" while affluent consumers remain strong. Watch net charge-off rates on the card portfolio vs. the Q4 2025 baseline; any acceleration in consumer NCOs is the primary downside risk.

- **Expense discipline — efficiency ratio and ROTCE target:** BAC targets an efficiency ratio below 60% and ROTCE of 15-16% for 2026. Q1 compensation accruals (driven by strong trading) could push expenses up; but BAC has been a consistent cost manager. Moynihan's stated goal is to demonstrate operating leverage — i.e., revenue growing faster than expenses.

- **Capital return / dividend:** BAC has been rebuilding capital ratios post-Basel III recalibration. Any update on buyback cadence or dividend policy would be a meaningful signal, especially given the stock's YTD underperformance.

## What Matters This Print

- **EPS:** $1.00-1.01 consensus (range: $0.92–$1.10). Q1 2025 actual was $0.90. The ~11% YoY growth bar is achievable with NII step-up and expense leverage.
- **Net Revenue:** $29.78B consensus. Q4 2025 was $28.37B; Q1 2025 was $27.37B. The YoY growth is driven by NII + trading.
- **Net Interest Income (NII):** Consensus ~$14.5-15.0B quarterly. Q4 2025 NII was ~$14.5B. BAC guided for 5-7% FY2026 NII growth (FY2025 NII was ~$58-59B, so FY2026 implies ~$62-63B or ~$15.5B/quarter on a ramping trajectory). Any Q1 number below $14B would raise concerns; above $15B would be a beat.
- **Global Markets Revenue (FICC + Equities):** FICC consensus ~$3.0-3.5B; Equities ~$1.8-2.0B. Watch for confirmation that Iran/rate vol drove outperformance vs. Q2 2025 levels.
- **Investment Banking Fees:** Consensus ~$1.4-1.6B. Q1 2025 was ~$1.5B. Management commentary on Q2 IB pipeline conversion is critical.
- **Provision for Credit Losses:** Consensus ~$1.5-1.8B. Q4 2025 was ~$1.5B. Any reserve build above $2B would be a bearish signal.
- **CET1 Ratio:** ~11.5-12.0% expected. Watch for any buyback acceleration commentary.
- **ROTCE:** Target 15-16%; Q1 should track at ~14-15% depending on trading.
- **Merrill Lynch Wealth Management:** Watch for fee revenue and NNA trends — net new assets and financial advisor retention are key long-term value metrics.
